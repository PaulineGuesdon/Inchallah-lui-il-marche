\section{Introduction on photoassociation}
\subsection{Preamble on scattering theory}

In the study of electron-nuclei interactions, the Born-Oppenheimer approximation provides a natural 
simplification. Due to the large mass ratio $\mu = m_p/m_e \simeq 1000$, the nuclei move on a much 
slower timescale than the electrons. One can therefore neglect the coupling terms between the nuclear 
and electronic kinetic energies, and solve the electronic problem on spherical coordinate system centered
in nucleus.\\
Once the nuclear coordinates are fixed, the effective potential experienced by the electrons inherits the spherical symmetry of the problem. 
This allows the total wavefunction to be factorized into a radial and angular part:

\begin{equation}
    \psi(r, \theta,\phi) = R_l(r) Y^m_l(\theta, \phi)
\end{equation}

where the spherical harmonics $Y^m_l(\theta, \phi)$ encode the angular dependance.
The Schrödinger equation then reduces to a one-dimensional problem which can be treated for each
partial wave l:
\begin{equation}
    -\frac{\hbar^2}{2\mu}\left(\frac{1}{r^2} \frac{d^2 R_l(r)}{dr^2}r^2 - \frac{l(l+1)}{r^2} 
    + \hat{V}(r)\right)R_l(r) = E R_l(r)
    \label{radial equation}
\end{equation}
This one-dimensional structure allows an analogy with the Landau-Zener framework, as will be 
discussed below. To extract the scattering properties of the system, one studies the asymptotic 
behavior of the solutions to this equation. At large distances, where the potential $\hat{V}(r)$
vanishes, the general solution for each partial wave $l$ takes the form \cite{dalibard_interactions_nodate}:
\begin{equation}
    R_l(r) \xrightarrow{r\to\infty} \frac{a_l}{r}sin(kr-\frac{l\pi}{2} + \delta_l)
    \label{solution at long r}
\end{equation}

where $a_l$ is a normalization factor and $\delta_l$ the phase acquired by the l-th partial wave 
due to the interaction potential.\\

The full wavefunction is recovered by summing over all partial waves. At large distances, this
superposition can be rewritten as an incoming plane wave and an outgoing scattered spherical wave:

\begin{equation}
    \Psi \xrightarrow{r\to\infty} e^{i\mathbf{k}\cdot \mathbf{z}} + f(\theta) \frac{e^{ikr}}{r}
    \label{incoming outcoming scattering wave}
\end{equation}

where $f(\theta)$ is the scattering amplitude which determines wheter the collision is elastic or
inelastic and $\theta$ is the angle between the incident and scattered wavevectors.\\

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\linewidth,angle=90,origin=c]{Chapitres/Engineering highly entangled system of photoassociated 87Sr atoms/images/incident_and_scattered_wave.jpeg}
    \caption{Caption}
    \label{fig:scattering_concept}
\end{figure}

Requiring continuity of the wavefunction across all regions imposes a constraint on the phase shifts.
At low momenta $k$ one finds:

 \begin{equation}
    \delta_l \propto k^{2l+1}
 \end{equation}

For s-waves ($l=0$), this leads to the definition of the scattering length:
\begin{equation}
    a = -\lim_{k\to 0} \delta_0/k
    \label{scattering length}
\end{equation}
which characterizes the effective range of the interaction in the low-energy limit.
To further understand how the atoms scatter, the scattered part $\Psi - e^{ikz}$ on \ref{incoming outcoming scattering wave}
is expressed in a spherical basis as a sum over all the partial waves:

\begin{equation}
    \Psi \simeq \frac{i}{2kr}\sum_{l=0}^{\infty}(2l+1)\left((-1)^l e^{-ikr} - S_l e^{ikr}\right)
    \label{1D schrodinger solution}
\end{equation}

with $S_l = e^{2i\delta_l}$ the scattering amplitude. \\

For a \textbf{conservative hamiltonian} $|S_l| = 1$ meaning $\delta_l \in \mathbb{R}$.
In this case the wavefunction is well a sum of incoming and
outcoming waves of same amplitude.\\

The elastic cross section representing the surface where the scope 
of the potential is effective writes as
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_{el} = \frac{\pi}{k^2}\sum_{l=0}^{\infty}(2l+1)|1-S_l|^2
    \label{sigma elastic}
\end{equation}

But we see in equation \ref{1D schrodinger solution} that
$|S_l|$ needs to be $<1$ to allow dissipation of the wavefunction.
Defining $\delta_l$ as a complex number satisfies this condition.

In this case the inelastic collisions cross section reads as
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_{inel} = \frac{\pi}{k^2}\sum_{l=0}^{\infty}(2l+1)(1-|S_l|^2)
    \label{sigma inelastic}
\end{equation}

In ultracold gases, only s-wave atoms can collide because they do not have 
enough kinetic energy to pass the potential barrier of higher angular momentum states
so $l=0$ and the scattering amplitude is
\begin{equation}
    S_0 = e^{2i\delta_0} \simeq 1 + 2i\delta_0
\end{equation}

By using the result of \ref{scattering length}
\begin{equation}
     1 + 2i\delta_0 \simeq 1 - 2ika
\end{equation}

Because $\delta_0$ is complex, a direct result of these equations is that we can write a general scattering length as
\begin{equation}
    a = a_{bg} + i a"
\end{equation}

At low temperatures we can re-express equations \ref{sigma elastic} and \ref{sigma inelastic} as
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_{el} = 4\pi|a|^2
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_{inel} = 4\pi|a"|^2
\end{equation}

A usual case of the elastic collisions is atoms in the ground 
state. For inelasticity photoassociation is a good example of
dissipative process where two atoms in the ground state are coupled to a molecular state by a laser.

\subsection{What is photoassociation}
Photoassociation (PA) is a process from which free atoms pair our bounded to each other through two-body
and three-body losses via a resonant laser. \\
In the basis $\{\ket{\psi_g}, \ket{\psi_e}\}$ of free atoms in a radial potential $V_g(r)$ and first molecular state 
$V_e(r)$ in the absence of the resonant laser the equation evolution of the system express as

\begin{equation}
    i\hbar \frac{d}{dt}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        \psi_g\\
        \psi_e
    \end{pmatrix}
    =
    \begin{pmatrix}
        -\frac{\hbar^2}{2\mu}\nabla + V_g & 0\\
        0 & -\frac{\hbar^2}{2\mu}\nabla + V_e 
    \end{pmatrix}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        \psi_g\\
        \psi_e
    \end{pmatrix}.
    \label{elastic evolution equation}
\end{equation}

Then a resonant laser is tuned to an internal state of $V_e(r)$ to bound the atoms
pair as presented in figure \ref{fig:PA_concept}
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\linewidth]{Chapitres/Engineering highly entangled system of photoassociated 87Sr atoms/images/PA_concept.png}
    \caption{Caption}
    \label{fig:PA_concept}
\end{figure}

This operation consists in adding inelastic collisions to the system. Then the
effective evolution equation of the coupled atoms is

\begin{equation}
    i\hbar \frac{d}{dt}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        \psi_g\\
        \psi_e
    \end{pmatrix}
    =
    \begin{pmatrix}
        -\frac{\hbar^2}{2\mu}\nabla + V_g & \Omega/2\\
        \Omega/2 & -\frac{\hbar^2}{2\mu}\nabla + V_e + \hbar \Delta -i\frac{\gamma_{mol}}{2} 
    \end{pmatrix}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        \psi_g\\
        \psi_e
    \end{pmatrix}.
    \label{elastic evolution equation}
\end{equation}\\
with $\gamma_{mol}$ the molecular spontaneous emission linewidth.\\

Then sum of partial waves determined in \ref{1D schrodinger solution}. are solutions of this hamiltonian.
We can deduce the phase $\delta_l$ (see \ref{solution at long r}) and so the scattering length are then modified.
The real part of $a$ encodes two-body losses rate whereas its imaginary part changes the collisions properties. In this 
case we can write an effective scattering length enclosing the background scattering
length $a_{bg}$ standing for elastic collisions without any coupling, $\delta a$ a
modification of it because of the coupling and still the inelastic part of it
\begin{equation}
    a = a_{bg} + \delta a + ia"
\end{equation}

One can express the atom number evolution depending on this linewidth as

\begin{equation}
    \frac{dN}{dt} = - \frac{\beta N^2}{V_e} - \Gamma N
    \label{equation one and two-body losses}
\end{equation}

where $V_e$ is the effective volume of the cloud $(2\pi )^{3/2}\sigma_x 
\sigma_y \sigma_z$ for a density Gaussian distribution, $\Gamma$ 
the one-body loss rate limited by vacuum quality and 2-body losses express as $\beta \propto \gamma_{mol}$
and is possible to determine by a fit.\\


Finally in a context of spectroscopy, to adress more bound states
one sweep the laser frequency from the free-atomic line to red detuned frequencies. 
The quantity that will testify there is two-body losses
is the atom number remaining in the trap after a photoassociation pulse. 
The molecule leaves the trap by acquiring kinetic energy from the binding energy.

PA is mostly used in Feschbach resonance field because determining the exact 
position of the vibrational states enables by changing the magnetic field
to tune the scattering length of the atoms and thus the interactions in the system.

\subsection{Parameters for PA study}
Many experimental parameters enable to maximize the two-body loss rate of the
photoassociated atoms. This rate $K_2$ introduced above can express
as
\begin{equation}
    K_2 = \frac{\beta}{V_e} = \frac{1}{k}\frac{\frac{1}{4}\gamma_{stim}\gamma_{mol}}{\Delta^2 +(\frac{1}{2}(\gamma_{stim} + \gamma_{mol})^2)}
\end{equation}\\

To understand what governs $K_2$, we need to look more closely at $\gamma_\text{stim}$
which encodes the coupling between the colliding atom pair and the PA laser. This rate is linear with the laser intensity, proportional to the 3D density $n$,
and to the probability of transitioning from $\ket{\psi_g}$ to
$\ket{\psi_e}$ the Franck-Condon factor:

\begin{equation} 
    F_{FC} = \Bigg|\int_0^{\infty} \psi_g(r) \psi_e(r) dr\Bigg |^2 
\end{equation}

This factor directly measures the overlap between the initial and final 
state wavefunctions, and therefore quantifies how efficiently the PA laser 
can drive the transition. The Condon radius is the internuclear distance at 
which this overlap is maximum, meaning in a classical picture this is where 
the atoms spend the most time — and thus where the photoassociation is most 
likely to occur.\\
Since $\gamma_{stim}$ depends on both the laser intensity and 
the density, these become the key experimental knobs to tune $K_2$. 
To make this more concrete and allow comparison with other experiments, 
the photoassociation rate is commonly re-expressed in the molecular 
community through an optical length:

\begin{equation}
    l_{opt} = \frac{\beta m}{16 h }
\end{equation}

$m$ the atomic mass of the atom. This single quantity captures the overall 
PA efficiency, and from this formula it is clear that the main experimental 
levers to increase the PA rate are the cloud density and the PA beam 
intensity — precisely what we will test in the experimental results section.\\

\textbf{From loss rate to molecular structure}\\
So far we have described the PA process from the perspective of atom losses.
But the molecules formed in this process have a rich internal structure that 
determines which transitions are accessible and how efficiently they can be 
driven — in particular through the Franck-Condon factor. Understanding this 
structure is therefore essential to optimise $K_2$ experimentally.\\

A first key property is the symmetry of the orbital wavefunction. For an
even wavefunction the exchange of the two atoms $A$ and $B$ does not change the sign of the 
wavefunction, as in left figures \ref{fig:u-g symmetry}. The potential is then qualified \textbf{gerade}.
The probability of finding electrons in between the two atoms is high compared to the 
\textbf{ungerade} potential where the odd wavefunction cancels the probability of finding electrons in between.

    \caption{u-g symmetry}
    \label{fig:u-g symmetry}
\end{figure}

While exciting a single atom is straightforward, identifying the correct 
molecular state to target is considerably more involved, since a large 
number of potentials and internal states coexist. To navigate this complexity, 
we now introduce the molecular quantum numbers that label these states and 
determine their accessibility.\\

\textbf{The good quantum numbers}\\
The total angular momentum of the molecule is $T = R + F = R + I + J$, $F$ being the 
total spin of the two atoms $f_1 + f_2$, $J = j_1+j_2$ encodes the spin-orbit 
coupling, and R is the rotational angular momentum of the molecule. 
The projection of the angular momenta onto the internuclear axis is:
\begin{equation}
    \Omega = \Lambda + \Sigma
\end{equation}

where $\Lambda$ is the projection of the orbital momentum of the molecule on the internuclear axis
and $\Sigma$ the projection of the momentum spin.
$M_T$ denotes the projection of $T$ onto a chosen quantization axis.\\

For the rotational quantum number there is a parity of the total wavefunction that needs to 
be fulfilled. In addition there are only s-wave collisions in cold gases then the part of the 
wavefunction should be odd the spin one antisymetric\\

\begin{equation}
    \ket{\Psi} = \left(\ket{\phi_e}_A \ket{\phi_g}_B + (-1)^R \ket{\phi_e}_B \ket{\phi_g}_A\right)
     \otimes\ket{\chi}
\end{equation}

$\ket{\phi_e}$ and $\ket{\phi_g}$ are the electronic wavefunctions of the two atoms A and B 
in ground and excited state respectively, $\ket{\chi}$ is the spin wavefunction.
This restricts the accessible values $R = 0, 2$ or $4$... which directly limits the number of rotational states that can be populated during photoassociation.

For $^1S_0$ - $^3P_1$ molecular state there is a strong spin-orbit coupling that couples its 
related momentum to the internuclear axis (\ref{fig:hund}), quantified by $\Omega$ and described as Hund's case 
(c) \\

    \caption{hund}
    \label{fig:hund}
\end{figure}

The experiment presented in the following section is photoassociation in the $F=11/2$ hyperfine
state. We will focus in this state to simplify the discussion.\\
Say we have one atome in the $^1S_0$ state and one in the $^3P_1$ state: we can have a total spin of $J = 0, 1$ 
or $2$. It gives possible values of total atomic angular momentum F:

\begin{align}
\text{For } J=0, &\quad F=\frac{9}{2} \\
\text{For } J=1, &\quad F=\frac{7}{2},\frac{9}{2},\frac{11}{2} \\
\text{For } J=2, &\quad F=\frac{5}{2},\frac{7}{2},\frac{9}{2},\frac{11}{2},\frac{13}{2}
\end{align}\\


We have one atom with $f_1=9/2$ from the $^1S_0$ state and one in the $^3P_1$ state with $f_2=11/2$.
The possible values of total angular momentum of the molecule are 
\begin{align}
\text{For } R=0, &\quad {T = 1, 2, 3... 10}\\
\text{For } R=2, &\quad {T = 1,2... 12}\\
\text{For } R=4, &\quad {T = 1,2 ... 14}
\end{align}

The initial $T^i = i_1+i_2$ with $R=0$ because we are in s-wave collisions. In respect with
the parity $p = +1$ the possible values of $T^i$ are $T^i = 0, 2, 4...$.\\
As for atoms there are selection rules for transitions from a free state to a molecular state
fixed by the angular momentum of the electrons. 
$\Delta J = 0, 1$ gives $\Delta T = 0, 1$ with $T=0 \rightarrow T=0$ forbidden.\\
In the end the accessible transitions in the first molecular excited state of the $f_1=11/2$ are
\begin{align}
\text{For } R=0, &\quad {T^f = 1, 2, 3... 11}\\
\text{For } R=2, &\quad {T^f = 1,2... 13}\\
\text{For } R=4, &\quad {T^f = 1,2 ... 15}\\
...
\end{align}\\
There are 981 combinations of accessible $(R,T^f)$ molecular external states and for each potential a numerous
internal state we are going to demonstrate briefly in the following.\\

\subsection{Internal energy states}

\subsubsection{WKB approximation}
WKB approximation is a semi-classical approach where plane waves $\psi(x) = \frac{C}{\sqrt(k(x))}e^{i\int_0^{\infty} k(x) dx}$ are solutions of the 1D Schrödinger equation
for two particles of eigenenergie $E$ in a potential $V(x)$

\begin{equation}
    \frac{-\hbar^2}{2\mu}\frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} + V(x)\psi = E\psi
    \label{basic bitch schrodinger}
\end{equation}

giving the expression of the wavevector $k$
\begin{equation}
    k = \frac{p}{\hbar} = \sqrt{2\mu(E-V(x))}
\end{equation}

The condition of validity $\frac{d\lambda}{dx} \ll 1$ tells that the atoms must follow adiabatically the potential
to adapt their movement to its variation. It means at really small $x$ of region I of figure
\ref{fig:pa-sakurai-wkb} this approximation is not valid.\\

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{Chapitres/Engineering highly entangled system of photoassociated 87Sr atoms/images/sakurai_WKB.png}
    \caption{Caption}
    \label{fig:pa-sakurai-wkb}
\end{figure}

At position $x=x_1$ and $x_2$ the momentum diverges which means that the plane wave approximation is not valid anymore.
In the region $I$ and $III$ the wave is evanescent with a phase $k = i\sqrt{2\mu(|E-V(x)|)}$. It is then usual the
average the potential as linear with $x$ around these turning points. \\

By reflection with the left barrier of the potential at $x=x_1$ the stationnary wave must come back with a $2\pi$ at 
$x_2$. By continuity of the solutions in $[0, +\infty[$ the phase tends to
\begin{equation}
    \int_{x_1}^{x_2} k(x)dx = (n+\frac{1}{2})\pi 
    \label{wkb surfaces}
\end{equation}

Then this formula enables to identify the solutions $E$ that satisfies
the condition $(n+\frac{1}{2})\pi $. These values are what we call the vibrationnal states
of a molecular potential.

\subsubsection{Leroy-Bernstein theory}
Leroy-Bernstein theory enables to identify the position of the less bounded states without knowing the behaviour
of the potential that could be complicated to estimate and solve numerically. This model is valid only close to
the dissociation limit occuring at long distance. Then we can approximate the potential as
\begin{equation}
    V(r) \xrightarrow[r \to \infty]{} D_e - \frac{C_n}{r^n}
\end{equation}
 
with $D_e$ the dissociation energy. For a vibrational energy state $\nu$ of energy $E_{\nu}$
we express the energy difference $\epsilon = D_e - E_{\nu}$ to inject it in \ref{wkb surfaces}
and find solutions of

\begin{equation}
    \int_{x_1}^{x_2} \sqrt{2\mu(\frac{C_n}{r^n}-\epsilon)} dr = (\nu_D-\nu +\frac{1}{2})\hbar\pi 
\end{equation}
with $\nu_D$ the dissociation number. After few calculations we finally obtain

\begin{equation}
    \epsilon = \left((\nu_D-\nu)\frac{\sqrt{\pi }\hbar\nu}{\sqrt{2\mu C_n^{2/n}}}\frac{\Gamma(\frac{3}{2} - \frac{1}{\nu})}{\Gamma(1-\frac{1}{\nu})} \right)^\frac{2\nu}{\nu-2}
\end{equation}

$\Gamma$ being the Beta Euler functions. This energy also writes as
\begin{equation}
    \frac{E_\nu - D_e}{\nu_D - \nu} = \left(\frac{\sqrt{\pi }\hbar\nu}{\sqrt{2\mu C_n^{2/n}}}\frac{\Gamma(\frac{3}{2} - \frac{1}{\nu})}{\Gamma(1-\frac{1}{\nu})} \right)^\frac{2\nu}{\nu-2}
\end{equation}

from which we determine the energy difference between the dissociation limit and one of the least bounded states $\nu$
of the molecule. 

\subsection{External energy states}
As seen in the subsection 3.1.1 in a molecular picture the hamiltonian express as 

\begin{equation}
    \hat{H} = \frac{\hat{p_r}^2}{2\mu} + \frac{\hbar^2}{2\mu r^2}R\left(R+1\right) +\hat{V}_{BO} + \hat{H}_{HF} 
    \label{general hamiltonian of molecule}
\end{equation}
It is possible to neglect some orders of electronic interactions depending on the Hund's case and the distance region
between the atoms.
In our case we approximate the potential to be as for $^{173}Yb$ in \cite{han_photoassociation_2018}
\begin{equation}
    \hat{V}_{BO} = \frac{-C_6}{r^6}\left(1-\frac{\sigma^6}{r^6}\right) - s\frac{C_3^{\Omega}}{r^3}
    \label{ytt potential}
\end{equation}


\begin{equation}
\hat{H}_{\mathrm{hf}} =
A \, (\mathbf{i}_1 \cdot \mathbf{j}_1)
+
B \,
\frac{
3(\mathbf{i}_1 \cdot \mathbf{j}_1)^2
+ \frac{3}{2}(\mathbf{i}_1 \cdot \mathbf{j}_1)
- i_1(i_1+1)\, j_1(j_1+1)
}{
2 i_1 (2 i_1 - 1)\,
2 j_1 (2 j_1 - 1)
}
\end{equation}

$s=\pm1$ for gerade and ungerade potentials respectively.
The hyperfine term of the atom in $^1S_0$ is zero because its electronic angular momentum is zero.
Only the hyperfine interaction of the atom with $i_1, j_1$ in $^3P_1$ counts.

\subsection{Mass scaling ($^{88}Sr$)}
$^{88}Sr$ is a bosonic isotope of strontium with no hyperfine structure. The photoassociation
spectrum is then simpler than the fermionic ones : only two potentials in the first excited
molecular state $0_u$ and $1_u$. Results have been presented in \cite{zelevinsky_narrow_2006} 
where their fitting model gives the following potential expression
\begin{equation}
    \hat{V}_{BO} \sim \frac{-C_{6,\Lambda}}{r^6} -\frac{C_3}{r^3} + \frac{C_J}{r^2}
\end{equation}
for the two accessible potentials $\Lambda = 0, 1$. 

Mass scaling method presented in \cite{reschovsky_narrow-line_2018} enables to predict the position
of the vibrational states of the different isotopes of strontium. The authors consider the potential is the
same for all the isotopes because we can validate the Born-Oppenheimer estimation for all isotopes.
It is actually wrong to make this approximation in the case of ${87}^Sr$ because the hyperfine structure
modifies the expression of the potential. It is still giving an average position of the vibrational
states. Then for this method only the reduced mass in the integral

\begin{equation}
    \int_{x_1}^{x_2} \sqrt{2\mu(E-V(x))} dx = (n+\frac{1}{2})\pi 
\end{equation}

will change upon the isotope. Then with respect with the condition on the phase the energies $E$ 
will be modified. The experimental positions of the first vibrationnal states of the $O_u^+$ potential for $^{84}Sr$,
$^{86}Sr$ and $^{88}Sr$ are represented by the red crosses in figure \ref{fig:pa-mass-scaling} and enables to adjust
$V(r)$ shape. In blue lines the fitted functions are plotted from this model : it is then possible to deduce the position of the $^{87}Sr$ 
vibrationnal states thanks to the other isotopes lines.\\
We see discontinuites at the $84$ isotope: these are avoided crossing coming from a coupling
with the ground state potential.

\section{PA in practice}
\subsection{Protocol}
Dicke states are particularly interesting to engineer for metrology because it is possible to go
beyond quantum shot-noise limit on a specific parameter $\phi$. The strategy we performed
to attempt this high precision is to photoassociate atoms to induce two-body losses resulting
in single atoms entangled to the $N-1$ other.
The aim is to induce Dicke state on a gas of fermions with a precision that goes beyond quantum 
shot-noise limit. \\
As introduced in the preamble on scattering theory only s-wave collisions occur in ultracold gases.
It enforce that for fermions -by parity of the total wavefunction being antisymetric- 
only atoms with even angular momentum can collide meaning for PA process their spin state must be
antisymetric so is the case of $^{87}Sr$. For bosons it is the contrary: only atoms in triplet states
will be photoassociated by symetry of the total wavefunction.\\
Prior to the main experiment on $^{87}Sr$, we calibrated our protocol on the
$^{88}Sr$ isotope, whose photoassociation spectrum is well established in 
the literature \cite{borkowski_mass_2014}, \cite{reschovsky_narrow-line_2018}, \cite{zelevinsky_narrow_2006}.\\

PA rate highly depending on cloud density as discussed in 3.1.3 the first step for Dicke state 
engineering is to produce highly dense cloud managed differently depending on the fermionic or 
bosonic nature. \\
For fermions the convention to produce dense cloud is to optimize the evaporation ramps 
on a cloud already well charged in the ODT crossing.\\
The near-vanishing scattering length of bosonic strontium 88 is detrimental to evaporative 
cooling, as it severely limits the elastic collision rate required to thermalize the gas. Then

\subsection{Physical sources of inhabilitation of photoassociation}
\subsubsection{On F = 9/2 : predissociation}
On some cases PA process fails because of a phenomenon called pre-dissociation. 
When photoassociating a pair of free atoms from a potential X on a vibrational 
state from potential A it is possible that this last potential crosses another 
potential curve B, (e.g molecular potentials of other hyperfine states). Then it is possible that the excited molecule decays 
to a pair of quasi free-bound atoms in a potential B.\\
Considering it, it is reasonable to do PA on the lowest hyperfine state as in \ref{fig:pa-ytterbium-potentials},
in our case and in most of complex fermionic specie.
