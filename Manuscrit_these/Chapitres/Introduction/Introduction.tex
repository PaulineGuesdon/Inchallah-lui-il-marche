\subsection{Transverse cooling} 
The atomic beam is then collimated by transverse cooling (TC) using stationary waves propagating perpendicular to the atomic flux. In this way, the transverse 
velocity of atoms absorbing photons is reduced through the recoil momentum $\hbar k_\perp$, where $k_\perp$ is the transverse component of the TC wavevector. 
The intensity in the horizontal arm is set at least three times higher than intensity in the vertical arm ($\simeq 75I_{sat}$ on transition $^1S_0 \rightarrow ^3P_1$) 
since the microtubes of the oven are already constraining more the vertical velocity. This step last for $t_{BlueMOT} = 8.2~$sec meanwhile all the steps of the blue cooling stage.

The underlying mechanism is the Doppler cooling effect, as illustrated in Fig.~\ref{doppler shift}, where different atomic velocity classes are represented 
by Gaussian distributions centered at $\omega_0 \pm \vec{k} \cdot \vec{v}$, with $\omega_0$ being the atomic transition frequency. An atom moving at velocity 
$\vec{v}$ sees the laser frequency Doppler-shifted by $\pm \vec{k} \cdot \vec{v}$. Depending on the sign of this shift, atoms preferentially absorb photons 
from the counter-propagating laser beam, thereby slowing down their transverse motion.

\end{figure}

The optical frequency of this laser is set to $f_{\mathrm{TC}} = f_{\mathrm{Blue\,Master}} + f^{\mathrm{RF}}_{\mathrm{TC}}$, where $f^{\mathrm{RF}}_{\mathrm{TC}}$ 
is the RF driving frequency of the TC acousto-optic modulator (AOM). The laser frequency is experimentally optimized to be red-detuned from the targeted 
$^1S_0 \rightarrow {}^3P_1$ transition by $\Delta = -\frac{\Gamma}{2}$, where $\Gamma$ is the natural linewidth of the transition.

Increasing the TC laser power broadens the velocity capture range through power saturation, but also enhances recoil heating. Consequently, the cooling efficiency 
strongly depends on the laser detuning. As demonstrated in \cite{TC_thesis}, the number of collimated atoms exhibits a clear resonance-like dependence on this detuning, 
yielding an optimal value for $x$.

\subsection{Zeeman cooling}
Following the same momentum transfer principle, the atoms are subsequently decelerated along the longitudinal axis using a Zeeman slower. The underlying mechanism relies on 
a counter-propagating laser beam maintained on resonance with a velocity-dependent Zeeman transition as the atoms travel through a spatially varying magnetic field gradient 
generated by a set of coils.

shifted to $\omega' = \omega_L + kv$. To maintain the resonant absorption condition along the deceleration path, an inhomogeneous magnetic field $B(z)$ is applied to shift the 
atomic Zeeman sub-levels. As the atoms slow down, their Doppler shift decreases; the magnetic field profile $B(z)$ is thus tapered down to match the decreasing atomic velocity 
At the entrance of the slower, atoms possess a high mean velocity of $v_0 = 434$~m/s as detailed in the thesis of a previous PhD student of the group \cite{Bataille2020}. 
Due to the Doppler effect, an atom moving at velocity $v$ perceives the laser frequency $v(z)$, thereby sustaining the resonance condition:
\begin{equation}
\omega_L - \omega_0 + kv(z) - \frac{\mu_B g B(z)}{\hbar} = 0
\end{equation}

This spatial adaptation of $B(z)$ to $v(z)$ is physically equivalent to continuously adjusting the laser frequency for every new atomic velocity class after photon absorption. 

Since the magnetic field drops to zero at the center of the cell to avoid disturbing the downstream trapping region, atoms that have not reached the minimum capture velocity 
continue their trajectory through this zero-field zone. To catch these remaining fast atoms, additional coils with increasing radii are installed after the cell center. These 
coils create a supplementary magnetic field gradient that shifts the Zeeman levels back into resonance with the counter-propagating laser. Because the photons are absorbed 
strictly from the opposing laser beam, the resulting radiation pressure consistently acts to decelerate the atoms down to the desired capture velocity.

\subsection{Blue MOT}

\subsubsection{The physics}
The purpose of the Magneto-Optical Trap (MOT) is to trap atoms in a small volume while cooling them. The first stage consists of a "blue MOT" operating on the broad $^1S_0 
\rightarrow {}^1P_1$ transition at $461~$nm. This broad natural linewidth $\Gamma$ provides a large velocity capture range, making it ideal for capturing atoms coming from the 
Zeeman slower.

The MOT relies on a magnetic field gradient generated by a pair of coils in an anti-Helmholtz configuration, centered at the middle of the vacuum chamber, combined with three 
orthogonal pairs of counter-propagating laser beams at $461~$nm. For simplicity, the trapping mechanism is described here along the $x$-axis, as the behavior is symmetric in 
all three dimensions.

The process begins with a moderate magnetic field gradient $B(x) \propto x$. This linear position-dependent magnetic field shifts the atomic magnetic sub-levels via the Zeeman effect:
\begin{equation}
\Delta E(x) = - m_F g_F \mu_B B(x)
\end{equation}
This spatial energy shift is combined with counter-propagating laser beams carrying opposite circular polarizations ($\sigma^+/\sigma^-$). The light-matter interaction generates a 
spatial restoring force towards the trap center ($x = 0$) along with a damping friction force. As atoms are cooled and concentrated near the center, the magnetic field gradient is 
ramped up to compress the cloud.

During this stage, the gas temperature reaches the Doppler limit:
\begin{equation}
k_B T_D = \frac{\hbar \Gamma_{^1S_0 \rightarrow {}^1P_1}}{2}
\end{equation}
Due to the broad linewidth of the $^1S_0 \rightarrow {}^1P_1$ transition, this Doppler temperature ($T_D \approx 770~\mu\text{K}$) is much higher than the single-photon recoil 
temperature $T_r$.

\subsubsection{Hyperfine structure of Strontium-87}
Since we operate with fermionic Strontium-87 ($^{87}\text{Sr}$), which possesses a non-zero nuclear spin ($I = 9/2$), the electronic states exhibit a rich hyperfine structure. 
The ground state $^1S_0$ has $F = 9/2$, leading to 10 Zeeman sub-levels ($m_F = -9/2, \dots, +9/2$).

\subsection{Repumper}
To sustain the blue MOT cooling cycle, a repumping laser system is required. Although the $^1S_0 \rightarrow {}^1P_1$ transition is nearly closed, atoms in the $^1P_1$ state 
have a small decay branch into the metastable $^3P_2$ state (lifetime $\tau \approx 1000~$s) via the intermediate $^1D_2$ state. Without a repumper, atoms become dark to the 
$461~\text{nm}$ cooling light and are lost from the cooling cycle.

Repumping is provided by a $403~\text{nm}$ external-cavity diode laser (TOPTICA) incorporating an interference filter, which addresses the $^3P_2 \rightarrow {}^3D_2$ transition. 
To address the multiple hyperfine $m_F$ sub-levels (such as $m_F = 7/2, 9/2, 11/2$), the laser frequency is continuously swept over a range of $1~\text{GHz}$.

\subsubsection{Optimization of the repumper sweep}
To maximize atom recovery, the repumper frequency scan range is optimized around $1~\text{GHz}$. Scanning over a wider frequency range reduces the atom capture yield: a larger sweep amplitude reduces the dwell time on the specific hyperfine states responsible for the highest repumping efficiency. The modulation span is thus tuned to $1~\text{GHz}$ on the relevant $m_F$ sub-levels.

At the end of the blue MOT phase, the magnetic field gradient is strongly increased to compress the atomic cloud into a magnetic trap. The $461~\text{nm}$ cooling laser is then turned off while keeping the $403~\text{nm}$ repumper on. This ensures that dark atoms are fully repumped back into the ground state while remaining magnetically trapped, right before switching on the red MOT laser and adjusting the magnetic field.

\subsection{Red MOT}

\subsubsection{Optical setup}
Because the minimum temperature of the blue MOT is constrained by its large Doppler limit ($T_D \approx 770~\mu\text{K}$), further cooling requires transferring the atoms to a narrower optical transition. The second cooling stage operates on the narrow intercombination transition $^1S_0 \rightarrow {}^3P_1$ at $689~\text{nm}$, which has a natural linewidth of $\Gamma = 2\pi \times 7.4~\text{kHz}$. This narrow linewidth corresponds to a theoretical Doppler limit of $T_D = \frac{\hbar \Gamma}{2 k_B} = 179~\text{nK}$ and a photon recoil temperature of $T_r = 283~\text{nK}$.

The $689~\text{nm}$ light is provided by a commercial laser (Radiant Dyes) locked onto a high-finesse ultrastable optical cavity. The laser frequency is first detuned by \hl{$f = -600\text{ MHz}$} using a first acousto-optic modulator ("AOM Inj Slave 1") before injection into a master-slave amplifier system. A second double-pass AOM ("AOM 87") fine-tunes the frequency, after which the beam is divided into three pairs of counter-propagating arms for the three spatial axes of the MOT.

At the end of the blue MOT, the atomic cloud exhibits a spatial spread of $\sigma$ = \hl{value} and a temperature too high to be directly captured by a single-frequency red MOT (since the natural linewidth $\Gamma = 7.4~\text{kHz}$ is much narrower than the thermal Doppler broadening of the cloud). 

To capture these thermal atoms, a broadband MOT is first implemented on the $F=11/2$ hyperfine state of the $^3P_1$ level. The laser frequency is artificially broadened by applying a frequency modulation (sawtooth sweep) over a bandwidth $\Delta f =$ \hl{value}, red-detuned from the transition. As the atoms cool and compress into a smaller volume, the sweep amplitude $\Delta f$ and the laser power are progressively reduced.

The laser is set to a small fixed detuning $\Delta f =$ \hl{value} and a low intensity $I =$ \hl{value}. \subsection{Stirring laser ("Stir")}
In parallel with the red MOT, an additional "stirring" (or "stir") laser beam is applied to redistribute the magnetic sub-level population. Because the $^1S_0 (F=9/2) \rightarrow {}^3P_1 (F=11/2)$ cooling transition contains weak or non-absorbing Zeeman sub-states at the edges of the cloud, atoms trapped in these states experience reduced radiation pressure and escape the cooling cycle (Fig.~\ref{fig:intro-need-for-stir}).

The stirring laser addresses the $^1S_0 (F=9/2) \rightarrow {}^3P_1 (F=9/2)$ transition to optical-pump and randomize the $m_F$ populations, restoring efficiency to the main cooling cycle. The stir laser follows the exact same frequency modulation and power ramps as the main red MOT laser.

\subsubsection{Optimization of the narrow MOT parameters}
The cooling efficiency in the final single-frequency stage depends strongly on the laser intensity $I$ and detuning $\Delta$:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Power broadening: High laser intensity provides a large trap depth to capture atoms, but causes power broadening of the transition, where the effective linewidth increases as $\Gamma_{\text{eff}} = \Gamma \sqrt{1 + I/I_s}$ (where $I_s$ is the saturation intensity). This power broadening raises the achievable Doppler temperature limit. The intensity is therefore adiabatically ramped down to a value low enough to eliminate power broadening while remaining sufficient to overcome gravity.
    \item Detuning optimization: Setting the laser frequency too close to resonance leads to unwanted heating via spontaneous emission within the dense cloud. The central frequency and final detuning are experimentally optimized by minimizing the final cloud temperature.
\end{itemize}
\subsection{Optical Dipole Trap (ODT) and Evaporative cooling}

\subsubsection{Loading the crossed ODT}
The final cooling stage before optical trapping is the transfer of atoms into an Optical Dipole Trap (ODT) operating at $1061~\text{nm}$ (or $1075~\text{nm}$), followed by evaporative cooling. 

Atoms are loaded directly into the ODT from the single-frequency $689~\text{nm}$ red MOT. Because the ground state $^1S_0$ and the excited state $^3P_1$ have different dynamic polarizabilities ($\alpha_{^3P_1} > \alpha_{^1S_0}$ at $\sim 1064~\text{nm}$), the AC Stark shift induced by the dipole laser is state-dependent. As illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:intro-odt-loading}, the trap is deeper for $^3P_1$ than for $^1S_0$, resulting in a differential red-shift of the $^1S_0 \rightarrow {}^3P_1$ transition at the center of the trap. 

This differential shift creates a spatial resonance condition: atoms at the trap center remain in resonance with the red MOT beams, where they are continuously cooled and localized. Conversely, hot atoms at the periphery experience a negligible light shift and are blue-detuned from the local resonance, avoiding unwanted re-heating.

    \includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{Chapitres/Introduction/images/ODT_loading.png}

Following loading, forced evaporative cooling is performed to reach deep Fermi degeneracy, down to temperatures on the order of $T \approx 0.1\,T_F$ ($T \sim 100~\text{nK}$). Evaporative cooling is achieved by adiabatically ramping down the trap depth: the most energetic atoms overcome the potential barrier and escape the trap, while the remaining atoms re-thermalize to lower temperatures via elastic collisions (Fig.~\ref{fig:evap}).

\subsubsection{Optimization of the evaporation ramps: Dimple vs. Reservoir}
The crossed ODT consists of two orthogonal beams:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Vertical beam ("Dimple"): Focused to a small waist ($\omega_0^V = 34~\mu\text{m}$), this beam dictates the local trap depth and the tight confinement at the crossing, controlling the kinetic energy barrier for evaporation.
    \item Horizontal beam ("Reservoir"): With a much larger waist ($\omega_0^H = 147~\mu\text{m}$), this beam provides a large capture volume to hold the atomic reservoir and maintains high collision rates during the initial evaporation phase.
\end{itemize}

To compensate for gravity acting along the vertical axis, the intensity of the vertical beam is set \hl{$a$ times higher} than that of the horizontal beam. To reach quantum degeneracy, the intensity ramps of both beams are adiabatically optimized to maximize the phase space density $\propto \frac{N}{\bar{\omega}^3 T^3}$) while minimizing atom loss.

\section{Spin Selection}

\subsection{Optical Pumping}
Optical pumping is implemented prior to or during the final evaporation stages to prepare the gas in a tailored mixture of nuclear spin states ($m_F$). Due to the $SU(N)$ symmetry of $^{87}\text{Sr}$, elastic $s$-wave collisions between identical fermions in the same $m_F$ state are forbidden by Pauli blocking. Therefore, at least two distinct spin components must be present to enable elastic collisions and ensure efficient re-thermalization during evaporative cooling.

To perform state preparation, a bias magnetic field \hl{$B = \text{VALUE}\text{ G}$} is applied to lift the degeneracy of the $^3P_1, F = 11/2$ hyperfine sub-levels via the linear Zeeman effect. The atoms are illuminated by a $\sigma^-$-polarized optical pumping beam on the $^1S_0 (F=9/2) \rightarrow {}^3P_1 (F=11/2)$ transition. The frequency is set according to:
\begin{equation}
f_{11/2} = f_{\text{Radiant Dyes}} \pm f_{\text{Slave 1}}^{\text{RF}} + f_{11/2}^{\text{RF}}
\end{equation}
The laser frequency is swept across the targeted $^3P_1, F = 11/2, m_F'$ states to deplete specific ground-state populations in $^1S_0, F=9/2, m_F$. By spontaneous emission governed by dipole selection rules ($\Delta m_F = 0, \pm 1$), the atoms decay back to adjacent ground-state sub-levels ($m_F - 1$ and $m_F - 2$), progressively accumulating in the desired spin states.

To prepare an $SU(N)$ mixture (e.g., a 2-spin mixture), unneeded spin populations are sequentially pumped and blasted away, leaving the target states (such as $m_F = -9/2$ and $m_F = +9/2$) intact for collisional thermalization.

\subsection{Raman Transitions}

\subsubsection{Physical Concept}
The second chapter of this thesis focuses on spin manipulation within the $SU(10)$ manifold of $^{87}\text{Sr}$. This operation is realized via a two-photon Raman process driven by two laser beams red-detuned from the $^1S_0 \rightarrow {}^3P_1$ intercombination line.

Controlling the dimension of the effective Hilbert space from 1 to 10 requires independent addressing of individual spin states. The two-photon Rabi frequency $\Omega$ coupling two ground-state sub-levels $\ket{g, m_F}$ and $\ket{g, m_F'}$ through intermediate excited states $\ket{e}$ takes the form:
\begin{equation}
\hbar \Omega = \sum_{e} \frac{\bra{g, m_F'} \vec{d} \cdot \vec{E}_2^* \ket{e} \bra{e} \vec{d} \cdot \vec{E}_1 \ket{g, m_F}}{\Delta_e}
\end{equation}
where $\vec{d}$ is the dipole operator, $\vec{E}_1, \vec{E}_2$ are the electric field amplitudes, and $\Delta_e$ is the single-photon detuning relative to the intermediate state $\ket{e}$.

By applying the TLS, the energy difference between two consecutive spin projections $m_F$ and $m_F+1$ becomes non-linear:
\begin{equation}
\Delta E_{m_F}^{m_F+1} = q(2 m_F + 1) + \beta
\end{equation}
where $q$ parameterizes the quadratic (tensor) light shift and $\beta$ represents the linear Zeeman contribution. This explicit dependence on $m_F$ lifts the energy degeneracy between adjacent transitions, allowing spectral isolation and individual control of any given $m_F \leftrightarrow m_F+1$ pair.

\subsection{Fabry-Pérot Cavity and PDH Locking System}
The cavity length (distance between the two curved mirrors, $R = 99\%$) is chosen to establish spatial mode degeneracy. When locked to the laser, the cavity achieves a power transmission of $T = 0.54$ with a Free Spectral Range (FSR) of:
\begin{equation}
\text{FSR} = \frac{c}{2L} = 3~\text{GHz}
\end{equation}

The EOM generates sidebands at frequencies $f_0 \pm f_M$ around the carrier $f_0$, where $f_M = 40.48~\text{MHz}$ is the modulation frequency. The modulated electric field incident on the cavity is expressed as:
\begin{equation}
E_{\text{inc}}^{\text{mod}} = E_0 \left[ J_0(\beta) e^{i 2\pi f_0 t} + J_1(\beta) e^{i 2\pi (f_0 + f_M) t} - J_1(\beta) e^{i 2\pi (f_0 - f_M) t} \right]
\end{equation}
where $J_0(\beta)$ and $J_1(\beta)$ are the zeroth- and first-order Bessel functions of the first kind, with modulation index $\beta$.

The carrier enters the cavity and acquires a steep frequency-dependent phase shift near resonance, whereas the sidebands are promptly reflected from the input mirror. The interference between the reflected carrier and sidebands is detected on a fast photodiode, demodulated at $f_M$, and low-pass filtered to extract the dispersive error signal (Fig.~\ref{fig:pdh-error-signal}). The sharp slope around resonance provides a bipolar error signal sensitive to the sign of the frequency excursion.

    \caption{Typical Pound-Drever-Hall (PDH) error signal obtained by scanning the cavity length across resonance.}
    \label{fig:pdh-error-signal}

Digital feedback control was implemented using a Red Pitaya STEMlab board. The PID parameters were optimized by injecting electronic noise into the cavity piezo driver and measuring the closed-loop transfer function. The final feedback loop acting on the piezo crystal features a low-pass filter with a cut-off frequency $f_c = 40~\text{kHz}$, a proportional gain $1 < K_p < 10$, and an integrative gain $0 < K_i < 8~\text{s}^{-1}$.

\section{Spin Measurement Scheme}
Total atom numbers are determined via standard absorption imaging on the broad $^1S_0 \rightarrow {}^1P_1$ transition at $461~\text{nm}$ using a Vortex laser. State-resolved spin detection follows the momentum-transfer ("spin-kick") technique detailed in \cite{bataille_realisation_nodate}.

The measurement sequence proceeds as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Atoms are illuminated by two counter-propagating laser beams with opposite circular polarizations ($\sigma^+ / \sigma^-$), as illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:intro-spin-kick-scheme}(a).
    \item A state-selective pulse tuned to a specific $^1S_0 \rightarrow {}^3P_1$ transition adiabatically transfers targeted spin populations (Fig.~\ref{fig:intro-spin-kick-scheme}(b)).
    \item Each absorption-stimulated emission cycle imparts a coherent $2\hbar k$ momentum kick to the selected $m_F$ state.
    \item Following a time-of-flight (TOF) expansion duration $t_{\text{TOF}}$, spatial separation between spin components is observed in the absorption image (Fig.~\ref{fig:intro-spin-kick-scheme}(d)).
\end{enumerate}

Unaddressed spin states remain at the central position of the cloud. In contrast, states subject to the momentum transfer form distinct spatial wavepackets shifted to the left or right depending on the direction of the resonant momentum kick (e.g., $m_F = -3/2$ and $m_F = -7/2$ in Fig.~\ref{fig:intro-spin-kick-scheme}(d)).

To ensure efficient population transfer without parasitic Landau-Zener transitions, the optical pulse sweep rate must satisfy the adiabaticity criterion across the avoided crossing between coupled adiabatic states (Fig.~\ref{fig:intro-spin-kick-scheme}(c)).
