\subsection{Transverse cooling} 
There are then more collimated by Transverse Cooling (TC) stationnary waves that are propagating perpendicular to the atomic flux: this way the 
transverse velocity of the atoms absorbing photons is reduced by recoil energy $\hbar k_{\perp}$ with $k_{\perp}$ the transverse component of the TC electromagnetic
wavevector. The principle behind this cooling is the Doppler effect illustrated in Fig.\ref{doppler shift} where atoms with different speed classes are represented by the
different gaussian peaks centered in $\omega_0 \pm \vec{k}.\vec{v}$, $\omega_0$ the center pulsation of the atoms. The atoms of speed $v$ see the frequency $f_0 = 
2\pi \omega_0$ detuned by $\pm \vec{k}.\vec{v}$. Depending on the sign of this product the atoms see an effective frequency of the laser from which they absorb a photon. 
Then the emission of the same photon make them recoil by $\pm 2\hbar k_{\perp}$.

\subsection{Blue MOT}
\subsubsection{The physics}
The Magnetic Optical Trap (MOT) purpose is to trap the atoms in a small volume to cool them down. The first step of cooling is to do a blue MOT 
that uses the $^1S_0~-~^1P_1~,F=$ transition first to coincide the broad resonance with the broad Doppler shift velocity distribution.
This MOT is composed by a magnetic field gradient created by pairs of coils in the three directions, centered in the middle of a vacuum chamber and combined
to the same laser as before at $461$~nm. There is the exact same behavior in each dimension which is why - for simplicity - I will describe the phenomenon along
x only. 

One start with a small gradient at $B = ~G$ on atoms that are spread in a wide volume because of their kinetic temperature. This field expresses as $B \propto x$ 
which means that the fthe degeneracy lift $\Delta E = -m_F g \mu_B B$ is higher at extreme positions. 

It is combined with a counterpropagating beam that passes through a $\lambda /4$ waveplate to turn its polarization it's opposite circular. The interaction between 
light and matter takes the form of the radiation pressure that pushes the atoms with high velocity to the center of the B field: the more the velocity the stronger 
is this restoring force. At this level the laser is at a fixed frequency and the multiple spin projections are shifted also proportionnal to the position because 
the B field is linear with the position. Then when an atom is at position $a$ the laser is resonant with $m_F = -9/2$ and the atom looses $\hbar k$ energy that takes 
him close to the center. It becomes necessary to increase the gradient since the atoms are tending to smaller mean velocity closer to the $0^{th}$ position. 

The temperature of the gas attempt Doppler limit $k_B T = \frac{\hbar ~\Gamma_{^1S_0-^1P_1}}{2}$ that is colder than the recoil temperature.

\subsection{Raman transitions}
\subsubsection{Physical concept}
The second chapter of this thesis is about spin manipulation on SU(10) manifold. This operation is done by two-photons Raman process from two different lasers adressing
with a red detuning from the intercombination line. 

One want to control the size of the Hilbert space from one to ten which necessits the independant manipulation of the spin states. Raman transition on this line 
is the coupling of an $\ket{F, m_F}$ ground state manifold to a state from an ensemble of hyperfine states which has the form

\begin{equation}
    \hbar \Omega = E \times E^* \sum_{0}^{F = n} \frac{\bra{F, m_F'}\vec{d}.\vec{E}\ket{F, m_F}\bra{F, m_F}\vec{d}.\vec{E}\ket{F, m_F'}}{\delta}
\end{equation}

The energy difference between two consecutive states is simply 
\begin{equation}
    \Delta E_{m_F}^{m_{F+1}} = q(2 m_F +1) + \beta
\end{equation}

that shows the unique value of the energy difference between two consecutive states.

\subsection{Fabry-Perot cavity and PDH locking system}

The distance between the two curved mirrors composing the cavity is set for all the spacial electric modes to be resonant at the same frequency. It enables to maximize the transmitted intensity of the laser
through the mirrors of reflectivity R = 99$\%$. This cavity has a final transmission of $T = 0.54$ when locked on the laser for a Free Spectral Range
defined as $FSR = \frac{c}{2L} = 3~$GHz. 


The EOM of the PDH locking system creates sidebands around the center frequency of the laser $f_0 = f_{MOGlabs}$ at $f_0 \pm f_M$ 
with $f_M$ the modulation frequency of the EOM. Carrier and sidebands are interfering giving a modulated signal at the output of the EOM:
\begin{equation}
    E_{inc}^{mod} = E_0 \left(J_0(\beta)e^{if_0t}+J_1(\beta)e^{i(f_0 + f_M)t}-J_1(\beta)e^{i(f_0-f_M)t}\right)
\end{equation}
The first term at amplitude $J_0(\beta)$ - the $0^{th}$ order Bessel function - is the incident electric field at frequency $f_0$ with effective amplitude $E_0 J_0(\beta)$. 
The second and third terms come from the interference of the carrier with the RF signal giving rise to two sidebands at $f_0\pm f_M$ with amplitude $J_1(\beta)$ the first order Bessel function.
These side components are reflected from the first mirror and the carrier from the second acquiring a phase difference with the incident field. This reflection is measured by a photodiode 
which gives an error signal since it is mixed with the RF signal at $f_M = 40.48~$MHz and low-pass filtered. One can deduce from the modulated piezo of the cavity the error signal of shape 
Fig.\ref{pdh error signal} any derivation from the central frequency because of the strong slope. Then because the sidebands have opposite phase one can detect what side of the 
resonance the cavity is.

\section{Spin measurement scheme}
The atom number measurement is done by absorption imaging on the $^1S_0-~^1P_1$ transition with a Vortex laser at $461~$nm. The measurement of their spin state is detailed in our article \cite{bataille_realisation_nodate}.
The principle is the following: the atoms absorb photons from two counterpropagating lasers of opposite $\sigma$ polarization (as in Fig.\ref{fig:intro-spin-kick-scheme}(a)). One test a specific transition of $^1S_0-^3P_1$ 
that we want to measure (Fig.\ref{fig:intro-spin-kick-scheme}(b)). Then the atoms recoil by two momentum $\hbar k$ after the absorption and emission of the photon and are imaged after a 
time of flight of $t =$. Fig.\ref{fig:intro-spin-kick-scheme}(d) shows the final image of this process: at the center are the residual atoms that are not affected by the two lasers. Another small cloud
of atoms appears on the left of the central peak corresponding to $\ket{-3/2}$ population. At the opposite appears the $\ket{-7/2}$ from the same process that is affective on both way of the transition.
