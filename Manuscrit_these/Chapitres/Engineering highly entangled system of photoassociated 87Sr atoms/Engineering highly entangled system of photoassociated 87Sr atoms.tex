
\chapter{Engineering entangled ensembles $^{87}Sr$ by photoassociation}
In quantum metrology, a fundamental limit to the precision of a phase parameter $\phi$ is the \textbf{Standard Quantum Limit} (SQL), often referred to as quantum shot noise, defined as $\Delta\phi = 1/\sqrt{2Ns}$ \cite{pezze_quantum_2014}. In the previous chapter on Ramsey interferometry, we discussed how the achievable accuracy is often limited by experimental sources, such as atom leaking to other $m_F$ states affecting the signal-to-noise ratio.

To overpass this threshold, one must exploit non-classical states with specific correlations. For a collective spin state $|J, m\rangle$ the variance of one of the transverse spin components $\hat{J}_x, \hat{J}_y$ provides noise information of the enhanced interferometer.
The variance 
\begin{equation}
    \text{Var}(\hat{J}_x) = \frac{1}{2} \left( J(J+1) - m^2 \right)
\end{equation}

is maximized for the projection $m = 0$ with $J = NS$, $S$ the total spin. This configuration defines the \textbf{Dicke states}, powerful resources for quantum-enhanced metrology. The sensitivity of such a state is quantified by the \textbf{Quantum Fisher Information} (QFI). For a $m=0$ Dicke state, the QFI reaches:
\begin{equation}
    F_Q = 4 \text{Var}(\hat{J}_x) = 2NS(NS+1)
\end{equation}
According to the \textbf{Cramér-Rao inequality}, which relates the phase uncertainty $\Delta \phi$ to the QFI:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta \phi \geq \frac{1}{\sqrt{F_Q}}
\end{equation}
it becomes evident that the precision on $\phi$ is improved by a factor of approximately $\sqrt{N}$ compared to the SQL, scaling as $1/NS$, and approaching the \textbf{Heisenberg Limit}. This represents the ultimate precision allowed by the laws of quantum mechanics.

This chapter will demonstrate the generation and enhancement of Dicke states via the \textbf{photoassociation} (PA) process in $^{87}$Sr. This isotope is an ideal candidate for metrology due to its large nuclear spin and the high degree of control over its collective spin states. 

The discussion opens with fundamental elements of scattering theory. At large interatomic separations, the 1D Schrödinger equation yields to solutions that naturally define the scattering length, a key parameter for cold atom interactions. These concepts lay the groundwork for introducing photoassociation and the resulting atomic density evolution through one- and two-body losses. Subsequently, an analysis of molecular potentials—referred to here as external states—permits an estimation of the binding energies of the internal molecular states where two-body losses are enhanced. Finally, we present the experimental results and discuss the technical and physical challenges that could impact signal interpretation or lead to misidentification of the quantum enhancement.

\section{Introduction on photoassociation}
\subsection{Preamble on scattering theory}
Understanding the PA process necessitates an introduction to scattering theory to define key quantities such 
as the scattering length and the optical length. These parameters emerge naturally from the asymptotic analysis 
of the radial Schrödinger equation.\\
In the study of inter-atomic interactions, the Born-Oppenheimer approximation provides a natural
simplification. Due to the large mass ratio $\mu = m_p/m_e \simeq 1000$, the nuclei move on a much
slower timescale than the electrons. One can therefore neglect the coupling terms between the nuclear
and electronic kinetic energies, and solve the electronic problem on spherical coordinate system centered
in nucleus.\\
This allows the total wavefunction to be factorized into a radial and angular part:

\begin{equation}
    \psi(r, \theta,\phi) = R_l(r) Y^m_l(\theta, \phi)
\end{equation}
\\
where the spherical harmonics $Y^m_l(\theta, \phi)$ encode the angular dependance.
$R_l(r)$ is the function describing the radial movement of the bound atoms well described in the frame centered
in its center of mass. The potential experienced by the atoms inherits the spherical symmetry of the problem.
The Schr\"odinger equation then reduces to a one-dimensional problem which can be treated for each
partial wave $l$:

\begin{equation}
    -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m_p}\left(\frac{1}{r^2} \frac{d^2 R_l(r)}{dr^2}r^2 - \frac{l(l+1)}{r^2}
    + \hat{V}(r)\right)R_l(r) = E R_l(r)
    \label{radial equation}
\end{equation}

This one-dimensional structure allows an analogy with the \cite{Landau} framework, as will be
discussed below. To extract the scattering properties of the system, one studies the asymptotic
behavior of the solutions to this equation. At large distances, where the potential $\hat{V}(r)$
vanishes, the general solution for each partial wave $l$ takes the form \cite{dalibard_interactions_nodate}:
\begin{equation}
    R_l(r) \xrightarrow{r\to\infty} \frac{a_l}{r}sin(kr-\frac{l\pi}{2} + \delta_l)
    \label{solution at long r}
\end{equation}

where $a_l$ is a normalization factor and $\delta_l$ the phase acquired by the l-th partial wave
due to the interaction potential.\\
\newpage
Continuity of wavefunction for all inter-atomic distances imposes a constraint on the phase
shifts which at low momenta $k$ finds:

 \begin{equation}
    \delta_l \propto k^{2l+1}
 \end{equation}

For s-waves ($l=0$), this leads to the definition of the scattering length:
\begin{equation}
    a = -\lim_{k\to 0} \delta_0/k
    \label{scattering length}
\end{equation}
which characterizes the range of the interaction in the low-energy limit.

The full wavefunction is recovered by summing over all partial waves. At large distances, this
superposition can be rewritten as an incoming plane wave and an outgoing scattered spherical wave:

\begin{equation}
    \Psi \xrightarrow{r\to\infty} e^{i\mathbf{k}\cdot \mathbf{z}} + f(\theta) \frac{e^{ikr}}{r}
    \label{incoming outcoming scattering wave}
\end{equation}

where $f(\theta)$ is the scattering amplitude which determines wheter the collision is elastic or
inelastic and $\theta$ is the angle between the incident and scattered wavevectors.
\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{Chapitres/Engineering highly entangled system of photoassociated 87Sr atoms/images/diffusion_sinus.pdf}
    \caption[fig:scattering\_concept]{The wavefunction $\Psi$ at large inter-atomic distances $R(a_0)$ behaves as a summation of an incoming plane wave
    and an outgoing spherical wave with attenuated amplitude $f(\theta)$. Here is a representation of this wavefunction for s-wave
    collisions at $l=0$. The scattering occurs at background scattering length $R(a_o) = a_{bg}$ the distance
    where the potential is effective in absence of external coupling. }
    \label{fig:scattering_concept}
\end{figure}
The assymptotical expression of $\Psi$ and $e^{ikz}$ is expressed in a spherical basis as a sum
over all the partial waves. Then we can give as in \ref{incoming outcoming scattering wave} a decomposition of $\Psi$:
\begin{equation}
    \Psi \simeq \frac{i}{2kr}\sum_{l=0}^{\infty}(2l+1)\left((-1)^l e^{-ikr} - S_l e^{ikr}\right)
    \label{1D schrodinger solution}
\end{equation}

where we identify a spherical part and a scattering term, with $S_l = e^{2i\delta_l}$ the scattering amplitude.\\

In the presence of inelastic processes such as photoassociation, the colliding atom pair can be irreversibly lost
from the scattering channel---either by forming a bound molecular
state or by releasing kinetic energy. This loss of flux from the
incoming channel cannot be captured by a purely real phase shift,
as $S_l = 1$ implies full conservation of the wavefunction
amplitude. To account for this absorption of flux, $\delta_l$
must be extended to the complex plane, allowing $S_l < 1$
and introducing the possibility of inelastic scattering.\\

The associated elastic cross section representing the surface where the scope
of the potential is effective writes as
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_{el} = \frac{\pi}{k^2}\sum_{l=0}^{\infty}(2l+1)|1-S_l|^2
    \label{sigma elastic}
\end{equation}
But we see in equation \ref{1D schrodinger solution} that $|S_l|$ needs to be $<1$ to allow dissipation of the
wavefunction. Defining $\delta_l$ as a complex number satisfies this condition and the inelastic cross section
is

\begin{equation}
    \sigma_{inel} = \frac{\pi}{k^2}\sum_{l=0}^{\infty}(2l+1)(1-|S_l|^2)
    \label{sigma inelastic}
\end{equation}


Then from the condition $|S_l| < 1$ we deduce an approximated scattering amplitude $S_0$
\begin{equation}
    S_0 = e^{2i\delta_0} \simeq 1 + 2i\delta_0
\end{equation}

Added to the result of \ref{scattering length},
\begin{equation}
     1 + 2i\delta_0 \simeq 1 - 2ika
\end{equation}

Because $\delta_0$ is complex, a direct result of these equations is that we can write a general scattering length as
\begin{equation}
    a = a_{bg} + i a''
\end{equation}

At low temperatures we can re-express equations \ref{sigma elastic} and \ref{sigma inelastic} as
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_{el} = 4\pi|a|^2
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_{inel} = 4\pi|a''|^2
\end{equation}

As an illustration a usual case of the elastic collisions is atoms in the ground
state. For inelasticity photoassociation is a good example of
dissipative process where two atoms in the ground state are coupled to a molecular state by a laser.

\subsection{Photoassociation and collisions}
PA is a process from which free atoms pair are bound to each other. Effectively, the bound
molecule is typically seen as lost, and perceived as a two-body loss effect. \\
In the atomic pair frame, the electric dipole interaction drives the system from the ground electronic state
$\psi_g$ of potential $V_g(r)$ to the excited state $\psi_e$ of $V_e(r)$. \\
In the absence of the resonant laser the equation evolution of the system express as

\begin{equation}
    i\hbar \frac{d}{dt}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        \psi_g\\
        \psi_e
    \end{pmatrix}
    =
    \begin{pmatrix}
        -\frac{\hbar^2}{2\mu}\nabla^2 + V_g & 0\\
        0 & -\frac{\hbar^2}{2\mu}\nabla^2 + V_e
    \end{pmatrix}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        \psi_g\\
        \psi_e
    \end{pmatrix}.
    \label{elastic evolution equation}
\end{equation}

Then a resonant laser can be tuned to an internal state of $V_e(r)$ to bound the atoms
pair as presented in figure \ref{fig:PA_concept}
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth,trim=49.8mm 59.3mm 35.9mm 69.4mm,clip]{Chapitres/Engineering highly entangled system of photoassociated 87Sr atoms/images/Guttridge_PA_process.pdf}
    \caption{General concept of photoassociation from a free-atoms potential to an excited molecular state coupled by a resonant laser. Bound, the atoms have
    quantized accessible internal state described by the solution of 1D Schr\"odinger equation plotted of each vibrational state.}

    \label{fig:PA_concept}
\end{figure}

This operation consists in adding inelastic collisions to the system. Then the
effective equation evolution of the coupled atoms is

\begin{equation}
    i\hbar \frac{d}{dt}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        \psi_g\\
        \psi_e
    \end{pmatrix}
    =
    \begin{pmatrix}
        -\frac{\hbar^2}{2\mu}\nabla^2 + V_g & \hbar\Omega/2\\
        \hbar\Omega/2 & -\frac{\hbar^2}{2\mu}\nabla^2 + V_e + \hbar \Delta -i\frac{\gamma_{mol}}{2}
    \end{pmatrix}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        \psi_g\\
        \psi_e
    \end{pmatrix}.
    \label{inelastic evolution equation}
\end{equation}\\
with $\gamma_{mol}$ the molecular spontaneous emission linewidth, $\delta = \omega_e-\omega_L$ the 
detuning of the laser from the excited state and $\hbar \Omega/2$ the coupling.\\

At large distance the solution of this hamiltonian takes the form of a spinor $\ket{\psi_g} + \epsilon \ket{\psi_e}$
where the excited channel is weakly populated far from resonance. The sum of partial waves determined in \ref{1D schrodinger solution}
are a representation of $\ket{\psi _g}$ at long distance.
From this asymptotic form one extracts the scattering phase shift $\delta_l$ (see \ref{solution at long r}) and so the scattering length are then modified.
The imaginary part of $a$ encodes two-body losses rate whereas its real part changes the collisions properties. In this
case we can write an effective scattering length enclosing the background scattering
length $a_{bg}$ standing for elastic collisions without any coupling, $\delta a$ a
modification of it because of the coupling and still the inelastic part of it:
\begin{equation}
    a = a_{bg} + \delta a + ia''
\end{equation}

One can express the atom number evolution depending on this linewidth as

\begin{equation}
    \frac{dN}{dt} = - \beta N^2- \Gamma N
    \label{equation one and two-body losses}
\end{equation}

$V_e$ is the effective volume of the cloud $(2\pi )^{3/2}\sigma_x
\sigma_y \sigma_z$ for a density Gaussian distribution, $\Gamma$
the one-body loss rate limited by vacuum quality and 2-body losses.\\


Finally in a context of spectroscopy, to adress more bound states
one sweep the laser frequency from the free-atomic line to red detuned frequencies.
The quantity that will testify there is two-body losses
is the atom number remaining in the trap after a photoassociation pulse.
Different process can occur for the molecule to leave the trap: by spontaneous emission
it can come back to a scattering channel by spontaneous emission and gain enough kinetic
energy to leave the trap. Spontaneous emission can also lead to a deeply bound state in
the ground state potential.\\

PA process is mostly used in Optical Feshbach Resonance (OFR) experiments to control the type
of interaction in a Bose-Einstein Condensate that depends on the scattering length \cite{yan_controlling_2013}:
$a<0$ induce interactive interactions and $a>0$ repulsive. This interaction is modified by changing the 
the laser intensity $I$ and/or the detuning $\Delta$ of the laser with the bound molecular state knowing that $\delta a$ expresses as

\begin{equation}
    \delta a \propto \frac{I\gamma_{mol}\Delta}{\Delta^2 + (\gamma_{mol}^2)/4}
\end{equation}

\subsection{General scaling laws for PA}
We will discuss in this subsection the dependence of the PA rate $K_2$ on different parameters. This latter introduced above can express as \cite{blatt_systematic_2011}
\begin{equation}
    K_2 = \beta \times V_e = \frac{8\pi \hbar}{m_p}\times \frac{l_{opt}}{\Delta^2 +\frac{1}{4}(1+2k l_{opt})^2}
    \label{K2 complet}
\end{equation}

where $\Delta$ is the detuning of the laser from the molecular resonance and $m_p$ the mass of the nucleus.
The relation between the two-body rate $K_2$ and the fitting parameter $\beta$ demonstrates as:

\begin{gather}
    \frac{dN}{dt} = -\beta \iiint n^2(x,y,z) \, d^3r \label{eq:3.24} \\[1.5ex]
    \text{with } n(x,y,z) = n_0 \exp\left( -\frac{x^2}{2\sigma_x^2} - \frac{y^2}{2\sigma_y^2} - \frac{z^2}{2\sigma_z^2} \right) \label{eq:3.25} \\[1.5ex]
    \frac{dN}{dt} = -\beta n_0^2 \pi^{3/2} \sigma_x \sigma_y \sigma_z = -\beta \frac{N^2}{2\sqrt{2}V_e} \label{eq:3.26} \\[1.5ex]
    \text{where } V_e = (2\pi)^{3/2} \sigma_x \sigma_y \sigma_z \label{eq:3.27} \\[1.5ex]
    \text{Then } K_2 = \frac{\beta}{2\sqrt{2}V_e} \label{eq:3.28}
\end{gather}


in comparison with \cite{han_photoassociation_2018}.
For low temperature the De Broglie wavelength $\lambda_{dB} = \frac{h}{\sqrt{2m k_B T}}$ settles the negligibility of wavector $k$ in \ref{K2 complet}. 
Then the PA rate for a tuned laser
\begin{equation}
    K_2 = \frac{16h }{m_p}l_{opt}
\end{equation}
depends only on the \textbf{optical length} $l_{opt}$ which caracterizes the strength of the coupling between
the scattered atoms and the bound molecular state. Experimentally this quantity is tunable by the laser intensity I
since it is linear with:
\begin{equation}
    l_{opt} = \frac{\lambda^3}{16\pi c}\frac{f_{FC}}{k}f_{rot}I
\end{equation}

where $f_{rot} = 1$ for $0_u^+$ resonances and $f_{rot} = 2$ for $1_u$ resonances  \cite{reschovsky_narrow-line_2018}, 
$\lambda=689.45$ nm the wavelength of the transition. The amplitude of the PA rate also varies with the recovery of the
initial $\psi_g$ and final state $\psi_e$ wavefunctions through the Franck-Condon factor $f_{FC}$

\begin{equation}
    F_{FC} = \Bigg|\int_0^{\infty} \psi_g(r) \psi_e(r) dr\Bigg |^2
\end{equation}
\newpage

$K_2$ is maximized when at the Condon radius this recovery is maximum. In a classical picture 
this distance corresponds to the region wherethe atoms spend the most time and thus where the 
photoassociation is most likely to occur.

From this discussion we deduce an atomic evolution

\begin{equation}
    \frac{dN}{dt} =  -\frac{2^{11/2} h }{V_e m_p}l_{opt}N^2 - \Gamma N
\end{equation}

depending on the parameter $l_{opt}$ maximum for high laser intensity and atomic density $n_0= 1/V_e$.

\subsection{Handling multiple molecular potentials}
So far we have described the PA process as if there was only one molecular potential
from the perspective of atom losses.
But the molecules formed in this process have a rich internal structure that
determines which transitions are accessible and how efficiently they can be
driven in particular through the Franck-Condon factor. Understanding this
structure is therefore essential to optimise $K_2$ experimentally.\\

While exciting a single atom is straightforward, identifying the correct
molecular state to target is considerably more involving, since a large
number of potentials and internal states coexist. To navigate this complexity,
we now want to determine and enumerate the molecular potentials $V_e$ by introducing the molecular quantum
numbers that label these states to finally determine their possible transitions.\\

Confirming to the atomic case, the Born-Oppenheimer approximation allows to describe the movement of the electrons
separately from the nuclei, then \ref{radial equation} is valid for bound atoms. The aim of this part of the
exposé is to determine the shape of $V_e$ on what equation \ref{radial equation} depends.\\

In ultracold gases, only collisions in s-wave are impacted by the interaction potential,
because partial waves of higher $l$ mostly are reflected by the centrifugal barrier before crossing regions of
lower interaction orders. Then in respect with Pauli statistics the orbital wavefunction $\ket{\phi}$ being even
the spin wavefunction should be odd to conserve the non-symmetric nature of the total wavefunction $\ket{\psi}$.
For two atoms A and B possibly in spin state $\ket{\beta_1}$ or $\ket{\beta_2}$ the expression of total wavefunction

\begin{equation}
    \ket{\Psi} = \left(\ket{\beta_1}_A \ket{\beta_2}_B + (-1)^R \ket{\beta_1}_B \ket{\beta_2}_A\right)
     \otimes\ket{\phi}
     \label{wavefunction parity}
\end{equation}

constrains the \textbf{rotational quantum number $R$} to values $R = 0, 2$ or $4$... \cite{reichenbach_optical_nodate}
This first intrinsic molecular quantum number describes the rotational motion of the molecule on its center of mass.
For even $R$ the molecular potential associated is called \textbf{gerade}. Symmetrically odd value of $R$ gives the
denomination of \textbf{ungerade} potential. It means that the probability of finding electrons in between the two
atoms is high compared to the \textbf{ungerade} potential where the odd wavefunction cancels the probability of finding
electrons in between as reveals the figure \ref{fig:u-g symmetry}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{Chapitres/Engineering highly entangled system of photoassociated 87Sr atoms/proba_presence.pdf}
    \caption[fig:u-g symmetry]{Gerade/ungerade specificitie of the electronic wavefunction of electrons orbiting around nucleus A and B.
    Left : modulus square of the wavefunction $\left|\psi_-(z)\right|^2$ for odd parity of $R=1,3,5...$. The electrons have a non-zero
    probability of being found in between the two atoms contrarely to Right: for even parity of $R=0,2,4...$ the density probability is zero 
    in between the two atoms.}
    \label{fig:u-g symmetry}
\end{figure}

Another interaction playing a crucial role in determining the molecular structure is the spin-orbit coupling. In this context, the total angular
momentum of the molecule is defined as $T = R + F = R + I + J$, where $F$ denotes the total spin of the two atoms $f_1 + f_2$, $J = j_1+j_2$
accounts for the total electronic angular momentum and R is the rotational angular momentum of the molecule. \\

The projections of these angular momenta onto the internuclear axis are characterised by
\begin{equation}
    \Omega = \Lambda + \Sigma
\end{equation}

where $\Lambda$ represents the projection of the orbital momentum of the molecule on the internuclear axis
and $\Sigma$ the projection of the spin angular momentum. Furthermore, $M_T$ denotes the projection of $T$ onto a chosen quantization axis.\\

In particular case of the $^1S_0$ - $^3P_1$ molecular state of $^{87}Sr$, the spin-orbit interaction is sufficiently strong to couple the electronic angular momentum
to the internuclear axis. This regime is quantified by $\Omega$ and is appropriately described within the framework of Hund's case (c) \ref{fig:hund}.\\


\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{Chapitres/Engineering highly entangled system of photoassociated 87Sr atoms/images/hund_case_c.pdf}
    \caption[fig:hund]{Hund case (c) is a case where spin-orbit coupling dominates the interaction, resulting in a angular momenta of projection
    $\hbar \Omega$ in internuclear axis. In this case we took $R = 0$ as in many cases. }
    \label{fig:hund}
\end{figure}


Now all the specificites of molecular potential states exposed such as the u-g symmetry and the stable quantum number for bound atoms
I can \textbf{apply to the case of $^{87}Sr$} for which we will photoassociate atoms in the $F=11/2$ hyperfine state. \\

Say we have one atome in the $^1S_0$ state with maximum value $f_1=9/2$ and one in the $^3P_1$ state at maximum $f_2=11/2$: we can have
a total spin of $J = 1$ and possible values of total atomic angular momentum F:
\begin{align}
\text{For } J=1, &\quad {F = 1, 2, 3... 10}
\end{align}

The possible values of total angular momentum of the excited molecule are
\begin{align}
\text{For } R=0, &\quad {T = 1, 2, 3... 10}\\
\text{For } R=2, &\quad {T = 1,2... 12}\\
\text{For } R=4, &\quad {T = 1,2 ... 14}
\end{align}

As for atoms there are selection rules for transitions from a collisional state to a molecular excited 
state fixed by the angular momentum of the electrons.
$\Delta J = 0, 1$ gives $\Delta T = 0, 1$ with $T=0 \rightarrow T=0$ forbidden.\\
In the end the accessible transitions in the first molecular excited state of the $f_1=11/2$ are
\begin{align}
\text{For } R=0, &\quad {T^f = 1, 2, 3... 10}\\
\text{For } R=2, &\quad {T^f = 1,2... 10}\\
\text{For } R=4, &\quad {T^f = 1,2 ... 10}\\
\end{align}

with only even values of R in respect with wavefunction parity of \ref{wavefunction parity}.
Finally we estimate about 981 combinations of accessible $(R,T^f)$ molecular external states
which is a complex number of potentials to explore experimentally. We expect to have a spectrum
similar to the experiment on $^{173}Yb$ \cite{han_photoassociation_2018} realised in the intercombination
line where the momentum projection associated to the molecular potentials $0_u, 1_g, 1_u, 0_g$ (see figure \ref{fig:pa-ytterbium-potentials})
comes from the hyperfine structure. For this species it is indeed expected
to count 205 different configurations of $(T, F, R)$ for the excited molecular states on intercombination line with an
atomic potential written as \ref{ytt potential}.

    triplet of quantum numbers $(T,F,R)$. The spectral lines are identified by $S_{u/g}$ with $S$ the total orbital momentum of the molecule

    \label{fig:pa-ytterbium-potentials}

The high density of states expected for $^{87}Sr$ makes the task of spectral assignment particularly challenging. To move beyond a purely statistical 
estimation and accurately predict the energy levels of these 981 configurations, a precise modeling of the molecular interaction is required. 
This involves a description of the coupling between the rotation of the nuclear frame, the electronic degrees of freedom, and the hyperfine structure.
Following the molecular picture introduced in section 3.1.1, the energy of these states is governed by the Hamiltonian $\hat{H}$ which, for a Sr-Sr system, 
can be expressed as:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{H} = \frac{\hat{p_r}^2}{2\mu} + \frac{\hbar^2}{2\mu r^2}R\left(R+1\right) +\hat{V}_{BO} + \hat{H}_{HF}
    \label{general hamiltonian of molecule}
\end{equation}

where some electronic interactions orders has been neglected depending on the Hund's case. For $^{87}Sr$
we approximate the potential to be as for $^{173}Yb$:

\begin{equation}
    \hat{V}_{BO} = \frac{-C_6}{r^6}\left(1-\frac{\sigma^6}{r^6}\right) - s\frac{C_3^{\Omega}}{r^3}
    \label{ytt potential}
\end{equation}
\newpage

\begin{equation}
\hat{H}_{\mathrm{hf}} =
A \, (\mathbf{i}_1 \cdot \mathbf{j}_1)
+
B \,
\frac{
3(\mathbf{i}_1 \cdot \mathbf{j}_1)^2
+ \frac{3}{2}(\mathbf{i}_1 \cdot \mathbf{j}_1)
- i_1(i_1+1)\, j_1(j_1+1)
}{
2 i_1 (2 i_1 - 1)\,
2 j_1 (2 j_1 - 1)
}
\end{equation}

$s=\pm1$ for gerade and ungerade potentials respectively.
The hyperfine term of the atom in $^1S_0$ is zero because its electronic angular momentum is zero.
Only the hyperfine interaction of the atom with $i_1, j_1$ in $^3P_1$ counts. \\

Among these potentials, approximately 80 internal states of $^{173}Yb$ were measured. Atom loss 
spectra, recorded as a function of PA laser detuning, reveal the position of bound states relative 
to the atomic resonance $|^1S_0, F=5/2\rangle \to |^3P_1, F=7/2\rangle$. Near this resonance, atoms 
are illuminated for 30 ms at a low intensity ($I = 37$ mW/cm$^2$) to avoid power broadening, thus 
allowing the bound states closest to the dissociation limit to be resolved. At larger red detunings, 
the intensity is increased and the illumination time is optimized to maximize the depth of the loss.

Theoretically, these results are analyzed by plotting the approximated potentials and adjusting 
parameters such as the $C_n$ coefficients to match the observed vibrational states. Several scaling 
laws and approximations assist in this assignment: the WKB approximation provides a semi-classical 
description of the bound states, while the LeRoy-Bernstein scaling is a powerful approach to predict 
the position of consecutive lines near the dissociation limit without requiring the full knowledge of 
the potential shape. Finally, mass scaling across strontium isotopes, as demonstrated in \cite{borkowski_mass_2014}, 
serves as a robust tool to cross-check these predictions.

\subsection{Internal energy states}
\subsubsection{WKB approximation}

The Wentzel-Kramers-Brillouin (WKB) approximation is a semi-classical method used to estimate the 
vibrational eigenvalues of a diatomic system. By modeling the interatomic potential, the position 
of a first bound state can be determined, which subsequently enables the deduction of the following 
vibrational levels.

In this approach, the wavefunction is assumed to take the form of a modified plane wave 
$\psi(x) = \frac{C}{\sqrt{k(x)}}e^{i\int_{x_0}^{x} k(x') dx'}$. These waves are solutions 
to the one-dimensional Schrödinger equation for two particles with reduced mass $\mu$ and 
eigenenergy $E$:

\begin{equation}
    -\frac{\hbar^2}{2\mu}\frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} + V(x)\psi = E\psi
    \label{eq:schrodinger_1d}
\end{equation}

This leads to the expression of the local wavevector $k(x)$:
\begin{equation}
    k(x) = \frac{p(x)}{\hbar} = \frac{\sqrt{2\mu(E-V(x))}}{\hbar}
\end{equation}

The validity of this approximation is governed by the condition $\frac{d\lambda}{dx} \ll 1$, 
implying that the local de Broglie wavelength must vary slowly compared to the scale of the 
atoms' motion. This can be explicitly rewritten in terms of the potential gradient:

\begin{equation}
    \left| \frac{\mu \hbar \frac{dV}{dx}}{\left[2\mu(E-V(x))\right]^{3/2}} \right| \ll 1
    \label{eq:wkb_validity}
\end{equation}

This expression highlights two critical regimes where the semi-classical approach fails. 
First, near the classical turning points where $E \approx V(x)$, the kinetic 
energy vanishes and the wavelength diverges. Second, at short internuclear distances
(Region I in Fig. \ref{fig:pa-sakurai-wkb}), the steepness of the repulsive wall $|dV/dx|$ 
becomes too large for the atoms to follow the potential variations adiabatically. In these 
regions, connection formulas, typically using Airy functions, are required to match the 
solutions across the turning points.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{Chapitres/Engineering highly entangled system of 
    photoassociated 87Sr atoms/images/WKB_sakurai.pdf}
    \caption[fig:pa-sakurai-wkb]{Semi-classical approximation in an interatomic potential 
    $V(x)$. The energy level $E$ intersects the potential at the turning points $x_1$ and 
    $x_2$. In the classically allowed Region II, the wavefunction oscillates with an amplitude 
    proportional to $1/\sqrt{k(x)}$. In the forbidden Regions I and III, the wavefunction is 
    evanescent.}
    \label{fig:pa-sakurai-wkb}
\end{figure}

At positions $x=x_1$ and $x_2$, the momentum reaches zero and becomes imaginary as the energy 
level $E$ crosses the potential $V(x)$. In the classically forbidden regions I and III, the 
wavefunction is evanescent and characterized by the decay constant $\kappa(x) = \sqrt{2\mu(V(x)-E)}/\hbar$. 
Around the turning points, it is standard to linearize the potential to connect the oscillatory 
and evanescent solutions. Region I is generally not studied in detail for photoassociation since 
the repulsive wall of the potential is extremely steep and diverges at very short distances.

To ensure a physical stationary state, the phase accumulated by the wave as it reflects between 
the barriers at $x_1$ and $x_2$ must satisfy a quantization condition. By ensuring the continuity 
of the solutions over $[0, +\infty[$, the phase integral must follow:
\begin{equation}
    \int_{x_1}^{x_2} k(x)dx = \left(n+\frac{1}{2}\right)\pi
    \label{eq:wkb_quantization}
\end{equation}

This formula, known as the Bohr-Sommerfeld quantization condition, enables the identification of 
the discrete energy eigenvalues $E_n$ that satisfy the integral. these values correspond to the 
vibrational states of the molecular potential.

\subsubsection{Leroy-Bernstein scaling}


The LeRoy-Bernstein scaling law builds upon the WKB approximation to identify the position of the 
high-lying vibrational states near the dissociation limit. This model is particularly powerful as 
it relies solely on the long-range behavior of the potential, bypassing the need for a detailed 
description of the complex short-range interactions. 

Near the dissociation limit $D_e$, occurring at large internuclear distances $r$, the potential 
can be approximated by its leading long-range term:
\begin{equation}
    V(r) \approx D_e - \frac{C_n}{r^n}
\end{equation}

where $C_n$ is the long-range coefficient (typically $n=6$ for Van der Waals or $n=3$ for resonant 
dipole-dipole interactions). For a vibrational state $\nu$ with energy $E_{\nu}$, we define the 
binding energy as $\epsilon_{\nu} = D_e - E_{\nu}$. Substituting this into the Bohr-Sommerfeld 
quantization condition (Eq. \ref{eq:wkb_quantization}), we seek solutions for:

\begin{equation}
    \int_{r_{min}}^{r_{max}} \frac{\sqrt{2\mu}}{\hbar} \sqrt{\frac{C_n}{r^n} - \epsilon_{\nu}} \, dr = (\nu_D - \nu) \pi
\end{equation}

where $\nu_D$ is a non-integer parameter representing the effective vibrational index at the 
dissociation limit. After integration, the relationship between the binding energy and the 
vibrational quantum number is given by:

\begin{equation}
    \epsilon_{\nu} = \left[ (\nu_D - \nu) \frac{\hbar n}{2\mu^{1/2} C_n^{1/n}} \frac{\sqrt{\pi} 
    \Gamma(1/n + 1/2)}{\Gamma(1/n)} \right]^{\frac{2n}{n-2}}
\end{equation}

Here, $\Gamma$ denotes the Euler Gamma function. This expression is often rearranged to show the 
scaling of the vibrational spacing:
\begin{equation}
    E_{\nu} = D_e - \left[ (\nu_D - \nu) H_n \right]^{\frac{2n}{n-2}}
\end{equation}
where $H_n$ is defined as:

\begin{equation}
    H_n = \frac{\hbar n \sqrt{\pi}}{2\mu^{1/2} C_n^{1/n}} \frac{\Gamma(1/n + 1/2)}{\Gamma(1/n)}
\end{equation}

This notation highlights that the binding energy follows a power law relative to the "distance" 
from the dissociation limit, where the exponent is purely determined by the nature of the long-range 
interaction.

\subsubsection{Mass scaling and isotopic shifts}

Mass scaling is a powerful method used to predict the vibrational energy levels of different isotopes 
from a single known potential. By adjusting the reduced mass $\mu$ corresponding to the isotope considered, 
one can determine the phase constraint on the vibrational states. While this approach neglects the hyperfine 
structure, it provides a reliable estimation of the spectral range where vibrational resonances are expected.

In particular, $^{88}Sr$ is a bosonic isotope with a nuclear spin $I=0$, meaning it possesses no hyperfine 
structure. Its photoassociation spectrum is significantly simpler than that of fermionic $^{173}Yb$ (see Fig. 
\ref{fig:pa-ytterbium-potentials}), involving only two accessible potentials in the first excited molecular 
state: $0_u$ and $1_u$. Based on the results presented in \cite{zelevinsky_narrow_2006}, the potential can be 
modeled as:

\begin{equation}
    V(r) \approx -\frac{C_{6,\Lambda}}{r^6} -\frac{C_3}{r^3} + \frac{\hbar^2 R(R+1)}{2\mu r^2}
\end{equation}

for the two molecular symmetries $\Lambda = 0, 1$. Since the Born-Oppenheimer approximation remains valid 
across the strontium isotopes ($^{84}Sr, ^{86}Sr, ^{88}Sr$), the electronic potential $V_{BO}(r)$ is assumed 
to be identical for all species. Consequently, only the reduced mass $\mu$ in the WKB phase integral varies:

\begin{equation}
    \int_{x_1}^{x_2} \frac{\sqrt{2\mu(E-V(x))}}{\hbar} dx = \left(n+\frac{1}{2}\right)\pi
\end{equation}

The experimental positions of the first vibrational states for the $0_u^+$ potential in $^{84}Sr, ^{86}Sr$, 
and $^{88}Sr$ are shown as red crosses in Fig. \ref{fig:pa-mass-scaling}. These data points allow for the 
refinement of the potential shape $V(r)$. The fitted functions (blue lines) then enable the prediction of 
the frequency ranges where $^{87}Sr$ resonances should occur. Notably, discontinuities observed for the 
$^{84}Sr$ isotope correspond to avoided crossings resulting from couplings with the ground-state potential.

and $^{87}Sr$ with the appropriate experimental parameters.

\section{Photoassociation Experiments}

\subsection{Gas Preparation and Isotope Specificities}
The Photoassociation (PA) rate is strongly density-dependent, as detailed in Section 3.1.3. Consequently, the primary step for Dicke state engineering is the production of a high-density atomic cloud. The experimental approach differs according to the quantum statistics of the isotope:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Fermions ($^{87}$Sr):} High densities are achieved by optimizing evaporative cooling ramps once the atoms are loaded into the crossed Optical Dipole Trap (ODT).
    \item \textbf{Bosons ($^{88}$Sr):} The near-zero scattering length of $^{88}$Sr is detrimental to evaporative cooling, as it suppresses the elastic collisions necessary for thermalization. In this case, the final density relies almost exclusively on the efficiency of the preceding cooling and trapping stages (e.g., the MOT).
\end{itemize}

Once a dense cloud is obtained, the PA process is initiated by illuminating the atoms with a dedicated laser beam.

\end{equation}
The power ratio depends on the modulation depth $\beta$, as shown by the Bessel functions in Figure \ref{Bessel function}.

\subsection*{Conclusion: Validation of the PA Spectroscopic Protocol}
In summary, the characterization of our PA sources has highlighted two major technical constraints: the PDH-locking sidebands at $\pm 40.48$ MHz and the MOGlabs technical broadening (noise at $\pm 50$ kHz and $\pm 6$ MHz). However, the systematic analysis of $dN/dt$ dynamics and the cross-comparison with the "$11/2$ AOM" beam provide a robust diagnostic to isolate genuine molecular signals. With these technical limits established and the broadening mechanisms understood, we can now reliably analyze the molecular spectra for both $^{88}$Sr and $^{87}$Sr.
