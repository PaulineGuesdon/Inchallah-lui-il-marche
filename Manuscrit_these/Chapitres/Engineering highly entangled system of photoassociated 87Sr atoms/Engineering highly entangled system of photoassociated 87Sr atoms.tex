\section{Introduction on photoassociation}
\subsection{Preamble on scattering theory}
Understanding PA process with two atoms necessits an introduction on scattering theory to bring out quantites such
as optical length and scattering length.\\
In the study of inter-atomic interactions, the Born-Oppenheimer approximation provides a natural
simplification. Due to the large mass ratio $\mu = m_p/m_e \simeq 1000$, the nuclei move on a much
slower timescale than the electrons. One can therefore neglect the coupling terms between the nuclear
and electronic kinetic energies, and solve the electronic problem on spherical coordinate system centered
in nucleus.\\
This allows the total wavefunction to be factorized into a radial and angular part:

\begin{equation}
    \psi(r, \theta,\phi) = R_l(r) Y^m_l(\theta, \phi)
\end{equation}
\\
where the spherical harmonics $Y^m_l(\theta, \phi)$ encode the angular dependance.
$R_l(r)$ is the function describing the radial movement of the bound atoms well described in the frame centered
in its center of mass. The potential experienced by the atoms inherits the spherical symmetry of the problem.
The Schr\"odinger equation then reduces to a one-dimensional problem which can be treated for each
partial wave l:
\begin{equation}
    -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m_p}\left(\frac{1}{r^2} \frac{d^2 R_l(r)}{dr^2}r^2 - \frac{l(l+1)}{r^2}
    + \hat{V}(r)\right)R_l(r) = E R_l(r)
    \label{radial equation}
\end{equation}

This one-dimensional structure allows an analogy with the \cite{Landau} framework, as will be
discussed below. To extract the scattering properties of the system, one studies the asymptotic
behavior of the solutions to this equation. At large distances, where the potential $\hat{V}(r)$
vanishes, the general solution for each partial wave $l$ takes the form \cite{dalibard_interactions_nodate}:
\begin{equation}
    R_l(r) \xrightarrow{r\to\infty} \frac{a_l}{r}sin(kr-\frac{l\pi}{2} + \delta_l)
    \label{solution at long r}
\end{equation}

where $a_l$ is a normalization factor and $\delta_l$ the phase acquired by the l-th partial wave
due to the interaction potential.\\

Continuity of wavefunction for all inter-atomic distances imposes a constraint on the phase
shifts which at low momenta $k$ finds:

 \begin{equation}
    \delta_l \propto k^{2l+1}
 \end{equation}

For s-waves ($l=0$), this leads to the definition of the scattering length:
\begin{equation}
    a = -\lim_{k\to 0} \delta_0/k
    \label{scattering length}
\end{equation}
which characterizes the range of the interaction in the low-energy limit.

The full wavefunction is recovered by summing over all partial waves. At large distances, this
superposition can be rewritten as an incoming plane wave and an outgoing scattered spherical wave:

\begin{equation}
    \Psi \xrightarrow{r\to\infty} e^{i\mathbf{k}\cdot \mathbf{z}} + f(\theta) \frac{e^{ikr}}{r}
    \label{incoming outcoming scattering wave}
\end{equation}

where $f(\theta)$ is the scattering amplitude which determines wheter the collision is elastic or
inelastic and $\theta$ is the angle between the incident and scattered wavevectors.
\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{Chapitres/Engineering highly entangled system of photoassociated 87Sr atoms/images/diffusion_sinus.pdf}
    \caption[fig:scattering\_concept]{The wavefunction $\Psi$ at large inter-atomic distances $R(a_0)$ behaves as a summation of an incoming plane wave
    and an outgoing spherical wave with attenuated amplitude $f(\theta)$. Here is a representation of this wavefunction for s-wave
    collisions at $l=0$. The scattering occurs at background scattering length $R(a_o) = a_{bg}$ the distance
    where the potential is effective in absence of external coupling. }
    \label{fig:scattering_concept}
\end{figure}
The assymptotical expression of $\Psi$ and $e^{ikz}$ is expressed in a spherical basis as a sum
over all the partial waves enable to further understand how the atoms scatter. We can give a
decomposition of $\Psi$ as in \ref{incoming outcoming scattering wave}

\begin{equation}
    \Psi \simeq \frac{i}{2kr}\sum_{l=0}^{\infty}(2l+1)\left((-1)^l e^{-ikr} - S_l e^{ikr}\right)
    \label{1D schrodinger solution}
\end{equation}

that gives rise to a spherical part and a scattering term with $S_l = e^{2i\delta_l}$ the scattering amplitude.\\

In the presence of inelastic processes such as photoassociation, the colliding atom pair can be irreversibly lost
from the scattering channel---either by forming a bound molecular
state or by releasing kinetic energy. This loss of flux from the
incoming channel cannot be captured by a purely real phase shift,
as $S_l = 1$ implies full conservation of the wavefunction
amplitude. To account for this absorption of flux, $\delta_l$
must be extended to the complex plane, allowing $S_l < 1$
and introducing the possibility of inelastic scattering.\\

The associated elastic cross section representing the surface where the scope
of the potential is effective writes as
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_{el} = \frac{\pi}{k^2}\sum_{l=0}^{\infty}(2l+1)|1-S_l|^2
    \label{sigma elastic}
\end{equation}
But we see in equation \ref{1D schrodinger solution} that $|S_l|$ needs to be $<1$ to allow dissipation of the
wavefunction. Defining $\delta_l$ as a complex number satisfies this condition and the inelastic cross section
is

\begin{equation}
    \sigma_{inel} = \frac{\pi}{k^2}\sum_{l=0}^{\infty}(2l+1)(1-|S_l|^2)
    \label{sigma inelastic}
\end{equation}


Then from the condition $|S_l| < 1$ we deduce an approximated scattering amplitude $S_0$

\begin{equation}
    S_0 = e^{2i\delta_0} \simeq 1 + 2i\delta_0
\end{equation}

Added to the result of \ref{scattering length},
\begin{equation}
     1 + 2i\delta_0 \simeq 1 - 2ika
\end{equation}

Because $\delta_0$ is complex, a direct result of these equations is that we can write a general scattering length as
\begin{equation}
    a = a_{bg} + i a''
\end{equation}

At low temperatures we can re-express equations \ref{sigma elastic} and \ref{sigma inelastic} as
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_{el} = 4\pi|a|^2
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_{inel} = 4\pi|a''|^2
\end{equation}

A usual case of the elastic collisions is atoms in the ground
state. For inelasticity photoassociation is a good example of
dissipative process where two atoms in the ground state are coupled to a molecular state by a laser.

\subsection{Photoassociation and collisions}
Photoassociation (PA) is a process from which free atoms pair are bound to each other. Effectively, the bound
molecule is typically seen as lost, and perceived as a two-body loss effect. \\
In the atomic pair frame, the electric dipole interaction drives the system from the ground electronic state
$\psi_g$ of potential $V_g(r)$ to the excited state $\psi_e$ of $V_e(r)$. \\
In the absence of the resonant laser the equation evolution of the system express as

\begin{equation}
    i\hbar \frac{d}{dt}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        \psi_g\\
        \psi_e
    \end{pmatrix}
    =
    \begin{pmatrix}
        -\frac{\hbar^2}{2\mu}\nabla^2 + V_g & 0\\
        0 & -\frac{\hbar^2}{2\mu}\nabla^2 + V_e
    \end{pmatrix}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        \psi_g\\
        \psi_e
    \end{pmatrix}.
    \label{elastic evolution equation}
\end{equation}

Then a resonant laser is tuned to an internal state of $V_e(r)$ to bound the atoms
pair as presented in figure \ref{fig:PA_concept}
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\linewidth]{Chapitres/Engineering highly entangled system of photoassociated 87Sr atoms/images/Guttridge_PA_process.pdf}

    \caption{General concept of photoassociation from a free-atoms potential to an excited molecular state coupled by a resonant laser. Bound, the atoms have
    quantized accessible internal state described by the solution of 1D Schr\"odinger equation plotted of each vibrational state.}
    \caption[fig:PA\_concept]{General concept of photoassociation from a free-atoms potential to an excited molecular state coupled by a resonant laser. Bound, the atoms have
    quantized accessible internal state described by the solution of 1D Schrödinger equation plotted of each vibrational state.}

    \label{fig:PA_concept}
\end{figure}

This operation consists in adding inelastic collisions to the system. Then the
effective evolution equation of the coupled atoms is

\begin{equation}
    i\hbar \frac{d}{dt}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        \psi_g\\
        \psi_e
    \end{pmatrix}
    =
    \begin{pmatrix}
        -\frac{\hbar^2}{2\mu}\nabla^2 + V_g & \Omega/2\\
        \Omega/2 & -\frac{\hbar^2}{2\mu}\nabla^2 + V_e + \hbar \Delta -i\frac{\gamma_{mol}}{2}
    \end{pmatrix}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        \psi_g\\
        \psi_e
    \end{pmatrix}.
    \label{inelastic evolution equation}
\end{equation}\\
with $\gamma_{mol}$ the molecular spontaneous emission linewidth.\\

At large distance the solution of this hamiltonian takes the form of a spinor $\ket{\psi_g} + \epsilon \ket{\psi_e}$
where the excited channel is weakly populated far from resonance. The sum of partial waves determined in \ref{1D schrodinger solution}
are a representation of $\ket{\psi _g}$ at long distance.
From this asymptotic form one extracts the scattering phase shift $\delta_l$ (see \ref{solution at long r}) and so the scattering length are then modified.
The imaginary part of $a$ encodes two-body losses rate whereas its real part changes the collisions properties. In this
case we can write an effective scattering length enclosing the background scattering
length $a_{bg}$ standing for elastic collisions without any coupling, $\delta a$ a
modification of it because of the coupling and still the inelastic part of it
\begin{equation}
    a = a_{bg} + \delta a + ia''
\end{equation}

One can express the atom number evolution depending on this linewidth as

\begin{equation}
    \frac{dN}{dt} = - \beta N^2- \Gamma N
    \label{equation one and two-body losses}
\end{equation}

where $\beta = K_2/V_e$ in comparison with \cite{han_photoassociation_2018}. $V_e$ is the effective volume of the cloud $(2\pi )^{3/2}\sigma_x
\sigma_y \sigma_z$ for a density Gaussian distribution, $\Gamma$
the one-body loss rate limited by vacuum quality and 2-body losses\\


Finally in a context of spectroscopy, to adress more bound states
one sweep the laser frequency from the free-atomic line to red detuned frequencies.
The quantity that will testify there is two-body losses
is the atom number remaining in the trap after a photoassociation pulse.
Different process can occur for the molecule to leave the trap: by spontaneous emission
it can come back to a scattering channel by spontaneous emission and gain enough kinetic
energy to leave the trap. Spontaneous emission can also lead to a deeply bound state in
the ground state potential.\\

PA is mostly used in Feschbach resonance field because determining the exact
position of the vibrational states enables by changing the magnetic field
to tune the scattering length of the atoms and thus the interactions in the system.

\subsection{General scaling laws for PA}
We will discuss in this subsection the dependence of the PA rate on different parameters
of the two-body losses rate $K_2$. This latter introduced above can express as \cite{blatt_systematic_2011}


\begin{equation}
    K_2 = \beta \times V_e = \frac{4\pi \hbar}{2m_p}\times \frac{l_{opt}}{\Delta^2 +\frac{1}{4}(1+2k l_{opt})^2}
\end{equation}\\

where $\Delta$ is the detuning of the laser from the molecular resonance and $m_p$ the mass of the nucleus.
For low temperature the De Broglie wavelength $\lambda_{dB} = \frac{h}{\sqrt{2m k_B T}}$ settles the negligibility of wavector $k$. Then the PA rate for
a tuned laser
\begin{equation}
    K_2 = \frac{16h }{m_p}l_{opt}
\end{equation}
depends only on the \textbf{optical length} $l_{opt}$ which caracterizes the strength of the coupling between the
the scattered atoms and the bound molecular state. Experimentally this quantity is tunable by the laser intensity I
since it is linear with
\begin{equation}
    l_{opt} = \frac{\lambda^3}{16\pi c}\frac{f_{FC}}{k}f_{rot}I
\end{equation}

with $f_{rot} = 1$ for $0_u^+$ resonances and $f_{rot} = 2$ for $1_u$ resonances and $\lambda=689.45$ nm the wavelength
of the transition \cite{reschovsky_narrow-line_2018}. The amplitude of the PA rate also varies with the recovery of the
initial $\psi_g$ and final state $\psi_e$ wavefunctions through the Franck-Condon factor $f_{FC}$

\begin{equation}
    F_{FC} = \Bigg|\int_0^{\infty} \psi_g(r) \psi_e(r) dr\Bigg |^2
\end{equation}

PA rate is maximized when at the Condon radius this recovery is maximum. In a classical picture this is where
the atoms spend the most time â€” and thus where the photoassociation is most
likely to occur.\\

From this discussion we deduce an atomic evolution

\begin{equation}
    \frac{dN}{dt} =  -\frac{16h }{V_e m_p}l_{opt}N^2 - \Gamma N
\end{equation}

depending on the parameter $l_{opt}$ maximum for high laser intensity and atomic density $n_0= 1/V_e$.

\subsection{Handling multiple molecular potentials}
So far we have described the PA process as if there was only one molecular potential.
from the perspective of atom losses.
But the molecules formed in this process have a rich internal structure that
determines which transitions are accessible and how efficiently they can be
driven â€” in particular through the Franck-Condon factor. Understanding this
structure is therefore essential to optimise $K_2$ experimentally.\\

While exciting a single atom is straightforward, identifying the correct
molecular state to target is considerably more involved, since a large
number of potentials and internal states coexist. To navigate this complexity,
we now want to determine and enumerate the molecular potentials $V_e$ by introducing the molecular quantum
numbers that label these states and determine their possible transitions.\\

Confirming to the atomic case, the Born-Oppenheimer approximation allows to describe the movement of the electrons
separately from the nuclei, then \ref{1D schrodinger solution} is valid for bound atoms. The aim of this part of the
exposÃ© is to determine the shape of $V_e$ on what equation \ref{1D schrodinger solution} depends.\\

In ultracold gases, only collisions in s-wave are impacted by the interaction potential,
because partial waves of higher $l$ mostly are reflected by the centrifugal barrier before crossing regions of
lower interaction orders. Then in respect with Pauli statistics the orbital wavefunction $\ket{\phi}$ being even
the spin wavefunction should be odd to conserve the non-symmetric nature of the total wavefunction $\ket{\psi}$.
For two atoms A and B possibly in spin state $\beta_1$ or $\beta_2$ the expression of total wavefunction

\begin{equation}
    \ket{\Psi} = \left(\ket{\beta_1}_A \ket{\beta_2}_B + (-1)^R \ket{\beta_1}_B \ket{\beta_2}_A\right)
     \otimes\ket{\phi}
     \label{wavefunction parity}
\end{equation}

constrains the \textbf{rotational quantum number $R$} to values $R = 0, 2$ or $4$... \cite{reichenbach_optical_nodate}
This first intrinsic molecular quantum number describes the rotational motion of the molecule on its center of mass.
For even $R$ the molecular potential associated is called \textbf{gerade}. Symmetrically odd value of $R$ gives the
denomination of \textbf{ungerade} potential. It means that the probability of finding electrons in between the two
atoms is high compared to the \textbf{ungerade} potential where the odd wavefunction cancels the probability of finding
electrons in between as reveals the figure \ref{fig:u-g symmetry}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{Chapitres/Engineering highly entangled system of photoassociated 87Sr atoms/images/gerade_ungerade.png}
    \caption[fig:u-g symmetry]{u-g symmetry}
    \label{fig:u-g symmetry}
\end{figure}

Another interaction playing a crucial role in determining the molecular structure is the spin-orbit coupling. In this context, the total angular
momentum of the molecule is defined as $T = R + F = R + I + J$, where $F$ denotes the total spin of the two atoms $f_1 + f_2$, $J = j_1+j_2$
accounts for the total electronic angular momentum and R is the rotational angular momentum of the molecule. \\

The projections of these angular momenta onto the internuclear axis are characterised by
\begin{equation}
    \Omega = \Lambda + \Sigma
\end{equation}

where $\Lambda$ represents the projection of the orbital momentum of the molecule on the internuclear axis
and $\Sigma$ the projection of the spin angular momentum. Furthermore, $M_T$ denotes the projection of $T$ onto a chosen quantization axis.\\

In particular case of the $^1S_0$ - $^3P_1$ molecular state of $^{87}Sr$, the spin-orbit interaction is sufficiently strong to couple the elctronic angular momentum
to the internuclear axis. This regime is quantified bt $\Omega$ and is appropriately described within the framework of Hund's case (c).

Now all the specificites of molecular potential states exposed such as the u-g symmetry and the stable quantum number for bound atoms
I can apply to case of $^{87}Sr$ for which we will photoassociate atoms in the $F=11/2$ hyperfine state. \\

Say we have one atome in the $^1S_0$ state with maximum value $f_1=9/2$ and one in the $^3P_1$ state at maximum $f_2=11/2$: we can have
a total spin of $J = 1$ and possible values of total atomic angular momentum F:
\begin{align}
\text{For } J=1, &\quad {F = 1, 2, 3... 10}
\end{align}

The possible values of total angular momentum of the excited molecule are
\begin{align}
\text{For } R=0, &\quad {T = 1, 2, 3... 10}\\
\text{For } R=2, &\quad {T = 1,2... 12}\\
\text{For } R=4, &\quad {T = 1,2 ... 14}
\end{align}

As for atoms there are selection rules for transitions from a free state to a molecular state
fixed by the angular momentum of the electrons.
$\Delta J = 0, 1$ gives $\Delta T = 0, 1$ with $T=0 \rightarrow T=0$ forbidden.\\
In the end the accessible transitions in the first molecular excited state of the $f_1=11/2$ are
\begin{align}

\text{For } R=2, &\quad {T^f = 1,2... 10}\\
\text{For } R=4, &\quad {T^f = 1,2 ... 10}\\

with only even values of R in respect with wavefunction parity of \ref{wavefunction parity}.

\subsection{Internal energy states}

\subsubsection{WKB approximation}
WKB approximation will enable us in this section to estimate eigenvalues of an estimated pair of atoms in a harmonic
potential. The position of a first bound state for each internuclear
value enhance a deduction of the next lines. \\
This is a semi-classical approach where plane waves $\psi(x) = \frac{C}{\sqrt{k(x)}}e^{i\int_{x_0}^{x} k(x') dx'}$
are solutions of the 1D SchrÃ¶dinger equation for two particles of eigenenergie $E$ in a potential $V(x)$

\begin{equation}
    \frac{-\hbar^2}{2\mu}\frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} + V(x)\psi = E\psi
    \label{basic bitch schrodinger}
\end{equation}

giving the expression of the wavevector $k(x)$
\begin{equation}
    k = \frac{p}{\hbar} = \sqrt{2\mu(E-V(x))}
\end{equation}

The condition of validity $\frac{d\lambda}{dx} \ll 1$ tells that the atoms must follow adiabatically the potential
to adapt their movement to its variation \cite{atkins}. It means at really small $x$ of region I of figure
\ref{fig:pa-sakurai-wkb} this approximation is not valid.\\

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{Chapitres/Engineering highly entangled system of photoassociated 87Sr atoms/images/WKB_sakurai.pdf}
    \caption[fig:pa-sakurai-wkb]{Semi-classical approximation enables to find eigen energies $E$ of a hamiltonian describing a pair of atoms
    in an harmonical potential $V(x)$ of region II. From a solution it is possible to deduce the next vibrational states of this known
    potential.}
    \label{fig:pa-sakurai-wkb}
\end{figure}

At position $x=x_1$ and $x_2$ the momentum reaches zero then turnd imaginary.
In the region $I$ and $III$ the wave is evanescent with a phase $k = i\sqrt{2\mu(|E-V(x)|)}$. It is then usual the
average the potential as linear with $x$ around these turning points. \\

By reflection with the left barrier of the potential at $x=x_1$ the stationnary wave must come back with a $2\pi$ at
$x_2$. By continuity of the solutions in $[0, +\infty[$ the phase tends to
\begin{equation}
    \int_{x_1}^{x_2} k(x)dx = (n+\frac{1}{2})\pi
    \label{wkb surfaces}
\end{equation}

Then this formula enables to identify the solutions $E$ that satisfies
the condition $(n+\frac{1}{2})\pi $. These values are what we call the vibrationnal states
of a molecular potential.

\subsubsection{Leroy-Bernstein scaling}
Leroy-Bernstein scaling profits from the WKB approximation to identify the position of the less
bound states without knowing the behaviour of the potential that could be complicated to estimate.
This model is valid only close to the dissociation limit occuring at long distance. Then we can
approximate the potential as
\begin{equation}
    V(r) \xrightarrow[r \to \infty]{} D_e - \frac{C_n}{r^n}
\end{equation}

with $D_e$ the dissociation energy. For a vibrational energy state $\nu$ of energy $E_{\nu}$
we express the energy difference $\epsilon = D_e - E_{\nu}$ to inject it in \ref{wkb surfaces}
and find solutions of

\begin{equation}
    \int_{x_1}^{x_2} \sqrt{2\mu(\frac{C_n}{r^n}-\epsilon)} dr = (\nu_D-\nu +\frac{1}{2})\hbar\pi
\end{equation}
with $\nu_D$ the dissociation number of the very first bound state \cite{brown_rotational_nodate}. After few calculations we finally obtain

\begin{equation}
    \epsilon = \left((\nu_D-\nu)\frac{\sqrt{\pi }\hbar\nu}{\sqrt{2\mu C_n^{2/n}}}\frac{\Gamma(\frac{3}{2} - \frac{1}{\nu})}{\Gamma(1-\frac{1}{\nu})} \right)^\frac{2\nu}{\nu-2}
\end{equation}

$\Gamma$ being the Beta Euler functions. This energy also writes as
\begin{equation}
    \frac{E_\nu - D_e}{\nu_D - \nu} = \left(\frac{\sqrt{\pi }\hbar\nu}{\sqrt{2\mu C_n^{2/n}}}\frac{\Gamma(\frac{3}{2} - \frac{1}{\nu})}{\Gamma(1-\frac{1}{\nu})} \right)^\frac{2\nu}{\nu-2}
\end{equation}

from which we determine the energy difference between the dissociation limit and one of the least bounded states $\nu$
of the molecule. We see from this formula the facility to predict the position of the vibrational state $\nu$ without
knowing anything except the long-range behaviour of the potential.

\subsubsection{Mass scaling ($^{88}Sr$)}
Mass scaling is a method to predict from a know potential the position of the phase constraint
vibrational states by adjusting the reduced mass with the isotope considered. The hyperfine structure neglected, it is still a good estimation of the position
range of the vibrational.\\
$^{88}Sr$ is one of the bosonic isotope experimentally observed that presents no hyperfine
structure. The photoassociation spectrum is then simpler than the fermionic one alike the
$^{173}Yb$ (see figure \ref{ytt potential}): only two potentials in the first excited
molecular state $0_u$ and $1_u$. Results have been presented in \cite{zelevinsky_narrow_2006}
where their fitting model gives the following potential expression

\begin{equation}
    \hat{V}_{BO} \sim \frac{-C_{6,\Lambda}}{r^6} -\frac{C_3}{r^3} + \frac{C_J}{r^2}
\end{equation}

for the two accessible potentials $\Lambda = 0, 1$.

The authors consider the potential is the
same for all the isotopes because we can validate the Born-Oppenheimer estimation for $^{84}Sr$,
$^{86}Sr$ and $^{88}Sr$.
Then only the reduced mass in the integral
\begin{equation}
    \int_{x_1}^{x_2} \sqrt{2\mu(E-V(x))} dx = (n+\frac{1}{2})\pi
\end{equation}

will change upon the isotope. In respect with the condition on the phase, the energies $E$
will be modified. The experimental positions of the first vibrationnal states of the $O_u^+$ potential for $^{84}Sr$,
$^{86}Sr$ and $^{88}Sr$ are represented by the red crosses in figure \ref{fig:pa-mass-scaling} and enables to adjust
$V(r)$ shape. In blue lines the fitted functions are plotted from this model : it is then possible to deduce the frequency
ranges we expect to find the resonances of the $^{87}Sr$.\\
We see discontinuites at the $84$ isotope: these are avoided crossing coming from a coupling
with the ground state potential.
